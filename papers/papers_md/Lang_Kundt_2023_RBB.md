Collective ritual is one of the universals of the human behavioral repertoire, present in various
social formations, be it religious groups, violent gangs, or grassroots movements. However, the
omnipresence of collective ritual repeatedly puzzled evolutionary scholars because ritual participation requires substantial resource investments while seemingly not providing any apparent
immediate benefits. For instance, ritual participants directly waste resources such as in animal
sacrifices or threaten their lives during rites of passage where recruits undergo strenuous ordeals.
Even milder forms of ritual participation require allocating time and energy that could be dedicated
elsewhere. Why are people willing to voluntarily decrease their immediate well-being (as a proxy for
long-term fitness) by participating in collective rituals?
Given the overwhelming spatiotemporal and cross-cultural presence of ritual behavior, an evolutionary explanation would suggest that proportional benefits must offset these fitness costs. Following a long tradition of ritual studies (Durkheim, 1912; Robertson Smith, 1898), recent

2. The form and function of collective ritual


2.1. The problems of living in a group


The most important benefits of living in groups among primates are the defense against predators
and competing groups (Van Schaik, 1983). However, living in groups also poses challenges ranging
from simple coordination difficulties, over increased resource competition, to the risk of free-riding
and conflict between individual interests. In the primate taxa, where within-group interactions are
relatively intense (compared to other group-living species such as bovids), these issues intensify
with decreasing genetic relatedness (Hamilton, 1964) and decreasing recurrent interactions (Trivers, 1971), trends that are both characteristic of contemporary hunter-gatherers (Hill et al.,
2014). In other words, increasing group size poses challenges to collective action that hinges on
a reliable assortment of individuals with cooperative intentions.
An obvious candidate for the communication of cooperative intentions facilitating assortment
in H. sapiens is language, the development of which is often championed as the defining moment
of human evolution. However, as we show below, fully evolved language capacities substantially
lagged behind the onset of collective action problems in early Homo. An assumed precursor of

—
language gestural communication that relies on pantomime and pointing (Arbib et al.,
2008)—could address this issue, but the challenge is to explain how an intangible concept of
cooperative intention could be communicated using gestures. The second challenge is to explain
why this mode of communication would be preserved after the onset of symbolic

2.2. Collective ritual as a communication platform


We define ritual based on its formal and structural aspects that are cross-culturally less variable than
its content and may, therefore, hold the key to understanding the evolutionary dynamic behind the
ritual behavioral pattern. Specifically, we utilize the definition of ritual suggested by Rappaport
(1999, p. 24), who considered ritual to be “the performance of more or less invariant sequences
of formal acts and utterances not entirely encoded by the performers.” Rappaport recognized
that ritual often does not have any practical consequences on the state of the world (i.e., it is
non-instrumental) but instead offers a conventional (i.e., invariant and encoded by previous generations) and performative (i.e., visible, tangible, physical) way to communicate personal intentions
toward cooperative actions stipulated by the group (i.e., the intensity of performance inter-individually vary).
Inspired by Peirce’s semiotic theory, Rappaport argued that due to the necessity of performance,
ritual creates a causal relationship, that is, an index, between the material signifier (e.g., the number of
pigs sacrificed) and the immaterial significatum (e.g., the extent of commitment to help in subsequent
warfare). These indexical messages afford to unambiguously communicate the internal states of the
performer via conventionally established channels that the receivers understand. Indeed, intense
ritual performance cannot help but indicate intense support for the social order and its related cooperative stipulations, while tepid performance cannot help but indicate tepid support. Due to its
reliance on physical performance that often cannot be easily faked (e.g., initiation rituals), rituals
remain an important platform for reliable communication of intentions and cannot be fully substituted by symbolic communication where intentions can be lied about (Sosis, 2003). By publicly performing a ritual, group members acknowledge the existence and acceptance of social order, indicate
the amount of their commitment to the order, and perpetuate the order’s key contours.
Following Rappaport’s insights, we argue that this multivalent communicative function of ritual
behavior is characteristic of complex signaling systems where various signals form a functional unit
(Barker et al., 2019; Hebets et al., 2016). Collective ritual fits well the criteria for a signaling system
because the ritual signals are often redundant (e.g., repetitively confirming commitment to a religious order with the same utterance), degenerate (e.g., showing commitment by sacrificing money
and suffering pain in the same ritual), and pluripotent (e.g., signaling both commitment and artistic
creativity when piercing the body with decorated needles). However, to understand how this signaling system evolved to fulfill its central function highlighted by Rappaport (communication of cooperative intentions), we need to reverse-engineer the process of the system’s complexification. That
is, we need to identify signals forming the building blocks of the signaling system and trace their
origins to an exaptive pool (sensu Gould, 2002, p. 1271) of proto-ritual traits that evolution molded
into collective ritual.


2.3. Three types of signals


We start by defining a signal as a display that is selected for its effect on the behavioral pattern of the
receiver, which promotes adaptive benefits for both the signaler and receiver (paraphrased from
Hebets & Papaj, 2005). For a display to be a signal, it needs to convey information (e.g., cooperative
intention) that is otherwise hidden, would not be evident without such a display, and is understood
by a receiver. Importantly, to be a suitable candidate for the early transitional signal, this display
should function without reliance on symbolic communication. This latter condition is a crucial
qualifier if we assume that ritual evolved from indexical communication analogous to the fixed signals of non-human primates (e.g., sexual swelling indicating estrus).

While contemporary rituals blend symbolic and indexical communication where signals are
necessarily entangled in the dense networks of culturally specific meanings, we need to expose
those signals that could serve as transitory, communicative acts between fixed indices and purely
symbolic signs (Deacon, 1997, p. 72). Presumably, such signals would harness a temporal correlation between the ritualized communicative act and a subsequent behavioral pattern corresponding to a cooperative intention (Tomasello et al., 1997), allowing the receiver to form this association
and select an appropriate behavioral response (Rowe, 1999). In line with this presumption, these
signals would first function on the dyadic level (as in other non-human primate gestures) and
only later evolve to allow for one-to-many communication of cooperative intentions.
Here we identify three types of signals that interact to facilitate cooperative communication in
contemporary rituals but may have pre-dated symbolic communication: similarity signals, coalitional signals, and commitment signals (see Table 1). While similarity signals indicate shared interests between the signaler and the receiver, coalitional signals indicate membership in a specific
group/collaborative ensemble, and commitment signals indicate the graded obligation toward a
particular social contract. These cooperative signals move from the simplest indication of vaguely
aligned interests between the signaler and the receiver (e.g., willing to engage in joint action) over a
more flexible indication of partaking in a specific collective action with the receiver (willing to
engage in antelope hunt) to indicating the strength of commitment to collective action with the
receiver where free-riding is a threat (unequivocally will engage in antelope hunt).
Although all three signals interact as a functional unit to facilitate cooperative assortment in contemporary rituals (e.g., some commitment signals are also coalitional signals), we will treat them as
ideal types throughout the article to show how they might have co-evolved from a dyadic signalerreceiver relationship to create the signaling system called collective ritual. Importantly, these ideal
types refer to the original function of the signal corresponding to the then available neurocognitive
abilities. As we showed briefly in Table 1 and will show more below, with further evolution of neurocognitive structures, new signals, and cultural practices, the informative potential of these signals
would continually broaden (e.g., after the evolution of shared intentionality and recursion, groupspecific similarity signals would indicate not only similarity but also group membership). Before
providing evidence for each signal’s functional role in cooperative assortment, we need to supply
at least some illustrations of these signaling behaviors in the ethnographic record.

2.4. Ethnographic excursus


Among the!Kung San, the meeting of different camps is accompanied by several days of dancing in
a figure-eight pattern around the fire that “ … brings the camps together to resolve their conflicts”
(Katz, 1982, p. 38) as would be expected of similarity signals. Dance is the most important ceremony
for the!Kung San, accompanying puberty rites or hunting (with action-specific dance indicating
coalitional membership). The commitment signaling function of dance is especially pronounced
during healing ceremonies where dancers heal an ill or possessed person by entering trance,
which often leaves the dancers unconscious for several hours, or by setting their hair on fire and
similar dangerous and energetically demanding practices (Marshall, 1962). In this manner, the healers indicate the intensity of their willingness to help another person or the whole camp if the dance
is directed to generally ward off any evil.
Another example of a ritual comprising all three signals can be found in the initiation ceremony
of the Andamanese people (Radcliffe-Brown, 1922). During the ceremony, the boy’s body is
smeared with burned iron oxide that produces a red color to indicate his membership among
the initiates (coalitional signal), and his body is scarified (a mix of commitment and coalitional signals) while older men dance in synchrony (similarity signal) around the boy (Radcliffe-Brown,
1922, p. 100). Subsequently, the boy needs to abstain from certain foods for several weeks, and
his body is further scarified, indicating commitment intensity.
As a third example, the Sun Dance of the Blackfoot people offered a complex arena for signaling
status, chastity, as well as pro-group sentiments and commitments due to elaborate role division.
Lasting for several days, the Sun Dance was a gathering place for several bands that would exchange
crucial information and resources and dance in unison (simialrity signal; Wissler, 1918). For
example, women could take public vows of chastity in the Ceremony of the Tongues or become
a medicine woman, which, while prestigious, meant giving away most of the accumulated property
to others (commitment signal; Wissler, 1918, pp. 231–233). On the other hand, men could take several roles indicating their status and membership (e.g., in a war party – coalitional signal), the most
severe being participation in the Torture Ceremony (commitment signal). Participants’ bodies were
painted to denote their role in this ceremony, pierced with wooden skewers, and later tied to a central pole of the dance lodge, where participants danced until their flesh tore apart (Wissler, 1918,
p. 263).
These brief examples illustrate how each described signal fits into a general scheme of the collective ritual to inform other ritual participants about some hidden quality of the performer.
While the examples were limited, a more thorough probe into the ethnographic record should
identify analogous practices across the globe (e.g., using pigments to paint the body of warriors
in rituals preceding a battle was traditionally found across the Americas; Laubin & Laubin,
1989). [1] Note that these signals overlap and interact in the provided examples. We should expect
such interactions in a complex signaling system (e.g., synchronous dancing several hours before
a raid signals aligned interests, membership in the raiding party, and commitment to the raid by
the spent energy during dancing); nonetheless, we assume that their original evolutionary pathways
were distinct enough to warrant their study in isolation (as described in Table 1). Likewise, the provided examples indicate that ritual participants may signal much more than their cooperative intentions (e.g., status or wealth; Power, 2017a; Xygalatas et al., 2021), suggesting that these signals may
have evolved for other reasons than facilitating cooperation. We deal with these alternatives in section 5 after reviewing the evidence for rituals as signals of cooperative intentions.


2.5. Evidence for the signals’ function


2.5.1. Similarity signals
We presume that early similarity signals would take the form of mirroring the receiver’s movements
and gestures. There is ample evidence suggesting that mimicking the behavioral patterns of

participants stripped of any symbolic meaning positively affects their perceived similarity, and the
same applies to synchronous movement (for a review, see Lang et al., 2017). Since similarity positively affects the perception of mutual relatedness between the signaler and receiver, similarity signals also facilitate the perception of aligned interests, which is one of the factors affecting the
structure of inter-personal ties (McPherson et al., 2001).
Indeed, mimicry facilitates prosocial behavior towards the imitator (e.g., Müller et al., 2012), and
this function is pronounced in collective synchronous movements such as dancing (for a metaanalysis, see Mogan et al., 2017). Similarly, ritual activities that include exact synchrony (e.g., chanting) were shown to increase monetary contributions to a shared pool in an economic game more
than rituals without synchrony (Fischer et al., 2013). Ritualized synchronous activity also leads to

–
higher ingroup affiliation among children (4 11 years old) compared to unstructured group activity
(Wen et al., 2015).
Importantly, as predicted by gradual signal complexification, these similarity signals currently
interact with coalitional membership. For instance, participants tend to imitate more the behaviors
of members of their ingroup than their outgroup (Yabar et al., 2006). When ostracized from their
group, participants tend to increase their imitative behaviors to fit back into their group (WatsonJones et al., 2016). Likewise, infants spend more time (indicating surprise) looking at people who
affiliate after performing intentionally different arbitrary actions than looking at affiliating people
who performed the same action (Liberman et al., 2018).


2.5.2. Coalitional signals
We presume that early coalitional signals would take the form of ritualized gestures resembling a
specific cooperative activity and or the form of visual markings. The ritualized signal of intention
to participate in a joint action would communicate to the receiver that the signaler proposes to
share a specific common goal including the necessary coordinative background to reach that
goal (Tomasello, 2008). Ritual behavior and ritual markings are effective in this regard because
they protect the coalitional signal from ambiguity by enforcing binary distinction on otherwise continuous processes (a person either marks or does not mark their body to indicate membership in a
raiding party).
Ritually established relationships appear to be the best predictor of cooperative relationships
among the Ache and Hadza hunter-gatherers (Hill et al., 2014). Moreover, studies with children
showed that when two groups of preschoolers were taught a different ritual (group-specific play
with beads), they spent more time displaying the ritualized sequence of gestures to the ingroup
members compared to children in the control condition who played with the beads in their idiosyncratic way (Wen et al., 2020).
In line with signal complexification, having the abstract notion of group identity would imply a
shared coordination ground of multiple group members, and mutually signaling coalitional membership would facilitate quick and precise execution of larger collective tasks (Sterelny, 2012,
p. 142). Indeed, using visual reminders of ritual participation increases the rate of cooperative
exchange among anonymous co-religionists in economic games (Shaver et al., 2018), presumably
by implying a shared coordinative and cooperative ground.


2.5.3. Commitment signals
We presume that early commitment signals would take the form of self-inflicted pain and resource
waste before a dyadic risky joint action. [2] Since ritualized signals are often excessively costly, they
indicate a graded assurance of commitment to a joint action that is more reliable than abstract symbolic communication. This principle is formalized in the costly signaling theory (Grafen, 1990;
Zahavi & Zahavi, 1999), which holds that in situations where both the signaler and receiver have
conflicting interests but would benefit from an honest communication of unobservable traits,
such a signal would carry increasing production costs—relative to benefits—to secure its honesty.
Since uncommitted signalers are not willing to produce the highly costly signal because they

perceive the cost as too high or benefits as too low (Lang et al., 2022), paying the cost of the signal
and later garnering the associated cooperative benefits is perceived as profitable only for the committed individuals (Sosis, 2003). [3]

Regarding empirical support for commitment signals, paying costs to signal cooperative intentions is associated with larger contributions to the common pool in the public goods game (Lang
et al., 2022) and costly rituals are recognized as commitment signals by the receivers (Hall et al.,
2015). Moreover, toddlers are sensitive to the time and effort costs of others’ actions and can translate these costs into the moral domain (Jara-Ettinger et al., 2015), suggesting that costly action in a
cooperative context would be understood by both signalers and receivers as more binding from an
early age.
In line with the assumed signal complexification, costly rituals often appear in religious context
where they indicate commitment to supernatural deities that regulate social conduct (Chvaja et al.,
forthcoming). Costly religious acts were associated with being named as helping others in rural
South India (Power, 2017b), and taking part in the extreme ritual of Thaipoosam Kavadi in Mauritius positively predicted subsequent anonymous charitable donations to the local Hindu temple
(Xygalatas et al., 2013).


2.5.4. Summary of signal types
We outlined similarity, coalitional, and commitment signals as ideal types of signals that comprise
the signaling system of human collective ritual. We provided evidence that these signals are associated with cooperative intentions, are understood as such by receivers, and are acquired relatively
early during development. It follows that if the similarity, coalitional, and commitment signals
were ritualized at the dyadic level and gradually coalesced to form a stable trait of one-to-many
cooperative communication we call collective ritual, each of those signals had to be under directional selection corresponding to particular selection pressures (as outlined in Table 1).


3. Identifying the window for the evolution of rituals


To investigate the hypothesis that ritualized signals assorting cooperators evolved under directional
selection, a temporal window for the evolution of these signals needs to be identified. We compare
the cooperative signals possibly present in the last common ancestor of H. sapiens and chimpanzees
with the dating of these signals in the archaeological record. This comparison will estimate when
those signals might have originated in the hominin lineage. Having estimates of the earliest possible
presence of collective ritual should serve as a steppingstone to looking into the preceding history of
the genus Homo in search of the selective pressures and processes in the making of collective ritual.


3.1. Cooperative signals in non-human primates


The primate order displays a rich array of communicative signals, predominantly acoustic. However, those signals are mostly innate and inflexible displays that lack recursion, fitting into the category of fixed indices (Tomasello, 2008). While some vocalizations may be invented and later
ritualized, the less-frequent gestural communication is more flexible (albeit often paired with acoustic signals in apes). As in the assumed origin of ritualized signals in hominins explained in section
2.3, invented gestures originate from co-opting behavioral patterns from one context (e.g., foraging,
eating) to communicate different information (e.g., initiate grooming). Through the process of
ontogenetic ritualization (Tomasello et al., 1997), a dyad shortens its idiosyncratic string of gestures
to a simple gesture that later functions as a placeholder for the original complicated pattern (e.g.,
play initiation), eventually yielding group-specific communication convention (Fröhlich & Van
Schaik, 2020; Perry et al., 2003). Theoretically, such invented gestural patterns could be utilized
for cooperative communication, as outlined in the current paper.

3.1.1. Similarity signals in non-human primates
The most fitting example of similarity signals is chimpanzee pant-hoot chorusing, which represents
an intentional vocal signal that co-occurs with other affiliative behaviors, is performed by socially
preferred partners, and may be group-specific (Fedurek et al., 2013). Apart from this one instance of
vocal synchrony, the evidence for using imitative behavior as signals in non-human primates is
lacking, although there is suggestive evidence for the capacity to use imitation as affiliative signals.
For example, capuchin monkeys were shown to prefer human experimenters who mimicked them
as opposed to experimenters who did not (Paukner et al., 2009).


3.1.2. Coalitional signals in non-human primates
Generally, the recruitment of coalition partners in coalitionary primate species relies on innate
instead of invented signals, as well as on social grooming and previous coalitionary experience
(Fröhlich & Van Schaik, 2020; Watts, 2002). There are hints of socially learned signals among chimpanzees, such as the high-arm grooming; however, this behavior indicates rather a subordination of
the arm-holder than a coalitionary membership because the variation in grooming styles is traced to
matrilines rather than specific groups (McGrew, 2017). Similarly, white-faced capuchin monkeys
form social traditions related to group-specific modes of dyadic interaction, but these group-specific
modes are not used to discriminate between less-known interactants from other groups (Perry et al.,
2003).


3.1.3. Commitment signals in non-human primates
There is relatively rich evidence for commitment-like signals in non-human primates. For instance,
male Guinea baboons and macaques were observed to use genital fondling as a risky re-assurance of
the stability of their dyadic bond, and the intensity of this practice was positively correlated with the
social distance between the signaler and the receiver (Dal Pesco & Fischer, 2018). Among chimpanzees, behaviors such as testicle handling were recorded as re-assurance gestures (Bygott, 1974).
While these behaviors do not represent a direct fitness cost, the behaviors carry significant risks
by targeting specific body parts that are extremely sensitive. That is, making oneself vulnerable
to the attack of others serves as an honest signal of affiliative intentions (Bruner et al., 2017). Likewise, these signals are used to enforce an affiliative signal from others — accepting the aversive
stimuli (finger in mouth, testicle handling) is an index of positive relationship (Perry et al., 2003).


3.1.4. Summary of non-human primate research
While we identified several signals in non-human primates that structurally correspond to the three
human signals, the non-human primate signals are rather rudimentary and play only a minor role
in facilitating mutualistic cooperation. Notably, the identified signals were gestural (except for vocal
chorusing), dyadic, and while sometimes costly, this cost was not graded and only tentative. Since
the signals were detected across various primate taxa, it is possible that these early signals were in
place in the last common ancestor of the simian infraorder and, therefore, presented a pool of signals that have been further molded into ritualized cooperative signals in the hominin lineage (where
selective pressures on cooperative communication were hominin-specific; e.g., chimpanzees engage
mostly in low-stake collective action). However, while the reviewed evidence is supportive of this
view, a reconstruction of the phylogenetic tree of these signals would be needed to establish whether
the signals represent a synapomorphy or are the product of convergent evolution in various species
of the simian infraorder.


3.2. Archaeological evidence for cooperative signals


3.2.1. Similarity signals in the archaeological record
We argued that similarity signals such as movement mirroring or synchronous dance serve to
enhance the perception of aligned interests. However, since gestural similarity signals would

leave no archaeological record, we are left with depictions of such activities through cave art. The
oldest recorded dance scene has been uncovered in Borneo caves and dates back to 20 ka (Aubert
et al., 2018). Nevertheless, the neurocognitive machinery necessary to produce cave art is far
superior to the machinery assumed for similarity signals, thus substantially biasing these estimates
to younger dates.


3.2.2. Coalitional signals in the archaeological record
The oldest material remains of coalitional signals are related to the use of pigments since these
signals (as opposed to gestural signals) leave material records. Although pigment use, such as
red ochre, was documented across hunter-gatherer populations in various contexts, coalitional
signaling appears to be the most important function (Duarte, 2014). Pigment use was recorded
as early as 285 ka in Kenya (Deino & McBrearty, 2002). There are also indications of
pigment used in the Fauersmith industry from South Africa, dating back to 500 ka (Watts
et al., 2016).


3.2.3. Commitment signals in the archaeological record
Costly commitment signals might be found in intentional body modifications; however, these
modifications are ordinarily performed on the soft tissue, thus leaving us again only with artistic
depictions of these practices that date back to 30 ka (e.g., the pierced phallic statuette from Vogelherd; Angulo et al., 2011). Similarly, hand images found across Spain and France printed on cave
walls (dating back to 27 ka) that miss various finger segments may indicate costly commitment signals (McCauley et al., 2018).


3.2.4. Summary of the archaeological record
The oldest archaeological evidence for similarity and commitment signals dated only to the rich
symbolic behavior of the Upper Paleolithic and mainly was found outside Africa. We conclude
that the young material evidence testifies more about our ability to document the use of such signals
than about the ability of past hominins to produce them. Indeed, the oldest material evidence for

–
ritualized signals coming from Africa is pigment use (500 285 ka), which does not rely solely on
behavioral gestures or perishable material such as human soft tissue.


4. The evolution of collective ritual in the hominin lineage


To further examine the evolutionary pathways of cooperative communication, we now turn to
the evidence indicating the presence of the key neurocognitive mechanisms necessary for sending and decoding the three types of ritualized signals. This dating may provide a rough outline
of when each ritualized cooperative signal would be feasible in the hominin lineage (possibly
circumventing the bias in the archaeological record). To refine these estimates, we next investigate the available evidence for the primary selective pressures on collective action in the hominin lineage that would force to co-opt these neurocognitive mechanisms to serve the signaling
function.
Note that we focus solely on the evidence from the African continent in this review. Although
some of the examined evidence has earlier dates outside Africa, we remain conservative in not
assuming that this evidence indicates the earlier presence in Africa. Many examined traits would
be crucially dependent on specific ecologies and subject to convergent evolution. Thus, to establish
the pathways of traits preceding collective ritual in H. sapiens, we trace the presence of these traits in
African hominins (rather than assuming that these traits were brought to Africa by hominins evolving outside of that continent).

4.1. The evolution of mechanisms facilitating cooperative signals


4.1.1. Mechanisms underlying similarity signals
Similarity signals crucially rely on the ability to imitate others where the imitator needs to transpose
the movements of the imitatee onto their motor network (the correspondence problem). This function is supplemented by the mirror-neuron system (Gallese et al., 1996; Heyes & Catmur, 2020),
which translates the audio-visual inputs generated by the action of others into motor commands
and maps them onto the imitator’s pre-motor network. Crucially, imitation patterns may be modulated top-down by the medial prefrontal cortex, indicating that these patterns can be used as an
intentional signal (Wang & Hamilton, 2012). The ability to imitate others is further strengthened
by motor learning (Heyes, 2011) and mediated by greater mirror-neuron system activity, as seen
in professional performers observing their own movements (Calvo-Merino et al., 2006). A frequent
inter-personal imitation would therefore promote a recognition of aligned interests based on
enhanced perception of similarity (Krueger, 2013).
Importantly, high-fidelity imitation (more specifically overimitation) is crucial for social learning and is the cornerstone of human cumulative culture, largely absent in other non-human primate
species (Tennie et al., 2009). We can infer on the presence of high-fidelity imitation in the hominin
lineage by examining the onset of imitative social learning, which appears to be first necessary for
transmitting the toolmaking process of the Acheulean Industrial Complex (Shipton & Nielsen,
2015) that dates back to 1.76 Ma in West Turkana, Kenya (Lepre et al., 2011). The Acheulean industry involves causally opaque actions that are not intuitively related to the goal of the action and
require high-fidelity action imitation (Schillinger et al., 2015). Thus, with the onset of the Acheulean
industry and its spread across the African continent (earliest evidence from South Africa by 1.6 Ma;
Chazan et al., 2008), the presence of high-fidelity imitation might be assumed in H. ergaster. The cooption of the structures underlying overimitaiton for the purpose of signaling is further supported
by the cerebral innovations found in H. ergaster, especially in the regions that are used for communication in contemporary H.sapiens (Ponce de León et al., 2021).


4.1.2. Mechanisms underlying coalitional signals
Cognitive mechanisms facilitating coalitional signaling assume dyad-specific vocal or gestural
patterns at a minimum. Therefore, associative learning is a crucial ability for receivers to connect
the observed signal with the ensuing signaler’s action. On the signaler part, coalitional signals
necessitate shared intentions and, by extension, recursion. Shared intentions are crucial for
joint action because they allow actors to monitor each other’s performance and dynamically
adjust their actions to the actions of the other actor. Recursion describes the ability of individual
A to understand that she shares a communication code with an individual B and that individual
B has the same knowledge. [4] Shared intentions and recursion crucially rely on functional neural
structures traditionally identified as the theory of mind (Frith & Frith, 2006). While the basis of
action understanding likely depends on the exact mechanism translating audio-visual inputs of
the action of others (i.e., the mirror-neuron system), the identified neural structures employed
by the theory of mind to facilitate shared intentions and recursion are generally broader (Schurz
et al., 2014).
Importantly, while dyadic and triadic coalitions are observed in non-human primates, they are
not cooperative coalitions in the meaning that they would spring from shared intentions and cooperative participation in goal attainment. For instance, collective chimpanzee efforts such as red colobus hunts are driven by “impact” individuals initiating the action without necessarily corepresenting other actors’ roles (Gilby et al., 2015; c.f. Boesch, 2002). Moreover, chimpanzees do
not readily share the spoil of such hunts, and catch re-distribution relies on tolerated theft
(Wood & Gilby, 2017). Compared to chimpanzee coalitions, human coalitions recruited for collective actions are highly cooperative and involve multiple roles, co-representing others and their roles
and understanding shared goals (Sterelny, 2012, p. 76).

In the hominin lineage, large-game hunting is, apart from warfare, one of the most important
collective actions requiring coalitionary recruitment and may serve as an indicator of the presence
of shared intentions. A proxy for coordinated hunting (or other aggressive collective action) is a
toolkit dated to 500 ka that comprised hafted stone points, which likely functioned as spear tips
for hand-held weapons in Kathu Plain, South Africa (Wilkins et al., 2012). Notably, this indirect
proxy for collective action coincides with the first appearance of pigment use in South Africa discussed in section 3.2.2. However, hunting tools are more widely detected in the African record starting from 300 ka (Sahle et al., 2013), which would be a more conservative estimate.
Furthermore, one of the selective pressures for recursion would be teaching or teacher-apprentice relationship. That is, the ability to intentionally instruct another person on their activity (Sterelny, 2012). Although teaching might have been already necessary for the transmission of the
Acheulean industry, it is more widely assumed to be in place in the Middle Pleistocene due to
an increasing diversification of tool technology associated with new core reduction strategies to produce blades (dated to 550 ka in East Africa; Johnson & McBrearty, 2010). The subsequent transition
to the Levallois industry (which appeared periodically in geographically disparate regions until
widespread in Africa after 300 ka) was very likely transmitted using teaching (Lycett et al., 2016).


4.1.3. Mechanisms underlying commitment signals
The underlying neurocognitive mechanisms supporting sending commitment signals overlap to a
large degree with coalitional signals. However, commitment signals are more demanding to decode:
apart from understanding other people’s affordances (needed for coordination during joint action),
signal receivers need to understand the costliness of the performed act and weigh it against alternative behavioral choices that the signaler could have made (Bulbulia, 2009). That is, to understand
that other people have a set of behavioral choices from which they opted for the particular signal
intensity (Jara-Ettinger et al., 2015). Such imaginative capacity would be crucially dependent on
mental time travel capabilities needed for planning future actions. Importantly, experimental evidence shows that, in contrast to 4-year-old children, chimpanzees fail to secure a reward from two
alternative sources, suggesting that chimpanzees do not internally simulate alternative future outcomes (Suddendorf et al., 2017).
One source of evidence for hominin mental time travel relates to the transportation of materials
to distant foraging sites that indicates foreseeing the need to make tools in the future. While the
transport of tools recorded in the Oldowan industry is confined to < 10 km (Plummer, 2004; similar
to chimpanzees), it is only in the late Middle Pleistocene, around 300 ka, when long-distance transport (50 km) of obsidian as a raw material for knapping is documented in Kenya (Brooks et al.,
2018).
With the advance of mental time travel capabilities, similarity, coalitional, and commitment signals could extend from “here-and-now” situations where a signal is associated with an immediately
following behavioral pattern to more distant future actions (e.g., cutting forearm as a sign of
unequivocal support during tomorrow’s raid). Together with the ability to transpose intentions
from one domain (signaling behavior) to a different and temporally distant domain (future collective action), mental time travel capabilities would afford staged and regularly performed signaling
events associating ritualized signals with future collective action, which is to say, collective ritual.


4.2. The evolution of ritual under selective pressures on collective action


We identified the periods when the key neurocognitive mechanisms that facilitate each ritualized
cooperative signal might have been first present. However, if the ritualized signals evolved because
they adaptively promoted mutualistic cooperation between the sender and receiver, we need to
further show that there were selective pressures for cooperation in the hominin lineage that
would push using the original mechanisms in signal production. We surmise that these pressures
could have been constituted by both extrinsic factors such as environmental variability and the

associated faunal turnover (Potts, 2012) as well as intrinsic factors related to morphological, behavioral, and, later, cultural adaptations pressing on further innovations through feedback loops (e.g.,
better hunters live in larger societies that pressure further evolution of social cognition, which may
drive technological innovation in hunting, etc.). Against the backdrop of this complex causal web,
we will discuss the onset of primary selective pressures that may have triggered the evolution of
cooperative communication and, subsequently, collective ritual without exploring the feedback
loops between those pressures (which would constitute another study).


4.2.1. Evolution of similarity signals
To start, we dated the possible presence of early imitative skills that would afford similarity signals
back to ≏1.76 Ma, corresponding to the emergence of the Acheulean industry associated with
H. ergaster in Africa (section 4.1.1). This dating coincides with the prolonged variability of moist
and aridity cycles, which created substantial selective pressures on the adaptability of H. ergaster
to various environments as well as on the ability to extract more animal tissue as a reliable source
of calories (Patterson et al., 2019). Likewise, converging evidence from the comparison of cranial
size, type of habitat, and feeding patterns of H. ergaster with non-human primates and contemporary hunter-gatherers suggest that these hominins lived in a meta-group network of around 100

–
members with individual foraging bands comprising 15 28 individuals (Domínguez-Rodrigo
et al., 2019), which would constitute a novel selective pressure on cooperative communication
(compared to a smaller social network of H. habilis).
Since imitation was likely in place to copy the Acheulean tool-making process, similarity signals
such as movement mirroring or vocal chorusing might have been exapted from these imitative abilities (i.e., ability to precisely imitate another’s motor patterns) to imply aligned interests during an
increasingly more frequent interactions with less-related individuals. The inferred aligned interests
would ease inter-personal cooperation and sharing of cooperative spoils.


4.2.2. Evolution of coalitional signals
The early forms of the neurocognitive structures supporting coalitional signaling, as indicated by
coordinated collective action and teaching, were dated to 550 ka (section 4.1.2). Some evidence
of earlier coordinated actions outside of Africa as well as estimates of cranial capacity associated
with recursion (Gamble et al., 2014) suggest that these structures might have evolved already during
a prolonged wet-arid variability cycle between 1.1–0.9 Ma (Potts & Faith, 2015). Nonetheless, we
remain conservative and rely on the evidence for teaching since teaching would also pressure the
evolution of ostensive communication in which the teacher needs to assure learners’ attention (Csibra & Gergely, 2011). That is, we conjecture that it is more likely that coalitional signals would be
evolving in later hominin such as the African and Eurasian assemblage of fossils labeled as
H. heidelbergensis (Stringer, 2016).
One of the primary selective pressure on cooperative assortment in the African
H. heidelbergensis would be an increased reliance on collectively acquired meat (Galway-Witham
et al., 2019). Large-game hunting would constitute inter-dependence of group members who
needed each other to acquire more meat, especially if providing for individuals with other important roles such as childcare or basecamp defense (Tomasello et al., 2012). Coalitional signals might
have played a pivotal role by effectively grouping cooperative individuals for joint hunts or moving
and processing animal carcasses. However, given the geographical spread of H. heidelbergensis, it
should be assumed that this repertoire would periodically appear and disappear together with
the variable selective pressures in given environments.
The signaling repertoire could have been gradually stabilized by an evolving suite of cultural
technologies related to the establishment of home bases (Rolland, 2004), where ritualized cooperative signaling could be gradually woven into the fabric of everyday social life, allowing for its
coalescence into a complex signaling system. In this respect, a key cultural technology would be
fire use, as documented in the contemporary ethnographic record (Wiessner, 2014). Fire would

afford communal night events that might have included repetitive signaling toward multiple individuals. Regular staged performance of ritualized dyadic or triadic signals around bonfires would
trigger conformist social learning (copy behavior of the majority in a group; Henrich & Boyd,
1998) and allow for the origins of group-specific signaling traditions, that is, signals understood
by the whole group (affording one-to-many communication).
Of course, such collective performance need not be limited to cooperative communication.
Communal fires would enable the conventionalization of other activities such as stone-tool making,
cooking, or food processing, where one-to-many displays of complex procedures would allow for
their group-level standardization (relying on overimitation), effectively establishing traditions
with a normative appeal as well as social identity. However, in contrast to the conventionalization
of these mundane activities, the staged and repetitive performance of ritualized cooperative signals
(standardizing the signals into “one proper form”) would institute collective ritual as the public
platform for communicating cooperative intentions. Whereas fire use dates deep to the Lower Pleistocene (Gowlett & Wrangham, 2013), the earliest evidence for the habitual use of fire associated
with home base, which is key for our argument, comes from the Qesem cave in Israel at 400 ka
(possibly associated with H. heidelbergensis; Barkai et al., 2017). By 350 ka, fire use was widespread
in Africa (Macdonald et al., 2021).


4.2.3. Evolution of commitment signals
The presence of neurocognitive structures supporting successful decoding of commitment signaling
was estimated around 300 ka (section 4.1.3), in a period of the graded transition from
H. heidelbergensis to the archaic H. sapiens (Hublin et al., 2017). In the same period, megagrazers
in East Africa were replaced by smaller-bodied herbivores, and the frequent wet-dry cycles were
associated with resource patchiness and variable resource landscapes even at a scale of 20 km
(Potts et al., 2020). Resource unpredictability characteristic of this climate variability would
pressure the evolution of foresight to simulate possible future events and select the most secure
options. Together with regular signaling performance around a communal fire that would create
a robust associative relationship between the signal and future action (e.g., iconic depiction of a
hunt and the next-day hunt), commitment signals might have originated as a response to doubts
produced by internal simulation of others’ possible actions (especially in the case of less-known
others). Co-opting the reliability-securing function of costly communication from other domains
such as meat-sharing could have provided the necessary material anchoring for these newly formed
signals.
The newly evolved commitment signals would be essential in between-group contact (as is the case
with contemporary hunter-gatherers; Hill et al., 2014) where interactions with less-known others
would be imbued with free-riding possibilities and the stakes would be much higher (e.g., battles
between clans). Since cumulative cultural evolution leads to flexible adaptations to local environments, the variability of ecological zones characteristic of East Africa from 300 ka likely corresponded
to variable cultural (non-kin) groups that would interact in a relatively constrained geographical area
(Malinsky-Buller & Hovers, 2019). This between-group contact would gain importance with resource
scarcity indicated by the sudden decrease in the effective population size of African humans post
300 ka (Bergström et al., 2020). Likewise, this scarcity would increase competition for resources
with other groups, implying the increased likelihood of inter-group conflict, where reliable coalitions
with other groups (secured by commitment signals) would provide an adaptive advantage. Indeed,
there is evidence from contemporary hunter-gatherers showing that inter-tribal warfare is positively
associated with the intensity of commitment signals (Sosis et al., 2007).

5. Alternative explanations for the evolution of collective rituals


We proposed that collective ritual evolved as an adaptation to selective pressures for cooperative
communication, which enhanced the cooperative abilities of Pleistocene hominins. By synthesizing
evidence from multiple disciplines, we aimed to illustrate the confidence or lack thereof in such an
evolutionary scenario. In this section, we briefly discuss alternative scenarios for the evolution of
collective ritual.


5.1. Ritual as a facilitator of social cohesion


By focusing on the communicative aspect of collective rituals, we neglected that by participating in
collective rituals, people not only communicate their similarity, coalitional membership, and commitment to others but also effectively establish these qualities in themselves. Ever since Durkheim


RELIGION, BRAIN & BEHAVIOR 15


(1912), the effervescence of collective rituals (Bulbulia et al., 2013) as well as shared dysphoric
experiences of extreme ritual practices (Whitehouse, 2018) were thought to cement inter-individual
bonds. Thus, the evolutionary roots of collective ritual may lie in its ability to establish bonds rather
than communicate them (Rossano, 2010). While we concur that the social-bonding consequences
of ritual participation are noteworthy effects, we maintain that these effects are partially derived
from (a) the signaling function of collective ritual and (b) the blending of collective ritual with
other bonding technologies that need not be ritualized.
First, while ritual performance informs receivers about the performer’s qualities and internal
states, the performance also informs the performer. Indeed, the fact that the performer is one of
the crucial receivers of the ritual message was highlighted by Rappaport (1999) and elaborated in
the model of religious socialization through ritual (Sosis, 2003). By partaking in the ritual, participants inform themselves about their beliefs, status, and commitments, effectively influencing their
future cooperative behavior.
Second, establishing mutual ties via social grooming or play does not necessarily involve
invented communicative acts because these activities trigger the endogenous opioid system that
facilitates social bonding (Dunbar, 2012). The defining aspects of collective ritual, that is, exaggerated and repetitive performance of stereotyped gestures and vocalizations, directly point to the primacy of the communicative function of rituals (i.e., these aspects indicate a need for signal clarity).
This conjecture is further supported by the non-human primate ritualized behaviors where ritual
greetings test the existing mutual bonds rather than establish them (Perry & Smolla, 2020).
Nonetheless, once collective ritual was established as a repetitive, conventional performance, the
bonding activities might have been blended with the communicative function, leading to collective
performance such as synchronous dancing or chorusing that affect both perceived similarity and
bond strength (Lang et al., 2017; Weinstein et al., 2015).


5.2. The communicative pluripotency of collective rituals


While we favored the cooperative communication function of collective rituals in the main paper, it
is important to note that many different messages might be communicated because collective rituals
are complex signaling systems (Barker et al., 2019). Apart from similarity, coalitional, and commitment signals, ritual performance may signal material wealth and social status (Power, 2017a; Power
& Ready, 2018; Xygalatas et al., 2021) or even physical attractiveness (Van Slyke, 2017). While we do
not doubt that various messages are communicated through the ritual platform, we maintain that
the cooperative intention is the likely primary message communicated in collective rituals (see Sosis
et al., 2007 for a test between these hypotheses on ethnographic data). Indeed, some sexual selection
accounts assume that the communicated mate quality relates to the commitment to group norms
(Van Slyke, 2017), and there is preliminary supportive evidence (Xygalatas et al., 2022). Likewise,
while synchronous movement appears also in the mate-selection context, it is often a by-product of
conventional ways of parading physical prowess and attractiveness of individual performers rather
than an intentional signal.


5.3. Collective ritual as an extension of individual ritual


Ritual behavior may have first evolved as an individual idiosyncratic habit and only later expanded
to a collective practice. A potential candidate function for the primacy of the individual-level
account is the ritual’s anxiolytic effect (Karl & Fischer, 2018; Lang et al., 2015, 2020; Sosis & Handwerker, 2011). The ritual decrease of anxiety might have evolved to dampen the overly sensitive
anxiety subsystem (especially in the domain of uncontrollable events) by molding the product of
blindly copying causally opaque actions into an exapted trait (Lang & Chvaja, 2023). Since complex
cultural practices are often imitated without understanding the mechanistic function of individual
sub-steps, similarly complex action sequences might have been used to influence uncertain and

uncontrollable future events (Fessler, 2006). Indeed, contemporary hunter-gatherers’ pre-hunt or
pre-battle rituals are permeated with taboos and superstitions concerning the correct ritual action
securing success [5], which may decrease anxiety and improve performance (Damisch et al., 2010).
However, this account does not explain why collective rituals would be staged, public, and often
very costly performances and would need to assume relative safety of ancestral environments
(such that ritualized anxiety decrease would be adaptive) or posit a by-product scenario (Boyer
& Liénard, 2006).


5.4. Collective rituals as a spandrel


Following previous clarifications of the adaptationist program (Sosis, 2009), we defined collective
ritual, specified its function, and reconstructed its possible evolutionary history, including the
analysis of the exaptive pool of mechanisms and signals that may have been co-opted under the
specified selective pressures. This is not to say that all aspects of collective rituals and ceremonies
are adaptive, functional, or even affect performers. Indeed, the cumulative cultural processes would
ornament the crucial function with non-functional layers.
Nevertheless, we maintain that the presented evidence suggest that the core aspects of collective
ritual present a complex adaptation and that it is unlikely such a costly behavior would be maintained across present and past societies if non-functional. Having said that, we are open to a reanalysis of the presented evolutionary model following Gould’s conditions for identifying spandrels
(Gould, 2002, pp. 1256–1257), which would show that (a) collective ritual reliably appears as a spandrel of specific psychological mechanism across cultures, (b) variation in ritual occurrence is tightly
correlated with the variation of the underlying psychological mechanism, (c) collective ritual has the
same form but does not gravitate to the same context (e.g., cooperative action problem) across cultures (i.e., is randomly distributed across contexts), and (d) that ritual is suboptimally designed to
facilitate mutualistic cooperation.


6. Conclusion


This article opened with the bewildering omnipresence of ritual behavior, paraphrasing Rappaport’s (1999, p. 140) puzzlement over why humans “who can communicate with ease, efficiency,
and subtlety through language should also employ such an awkward, limited and expensive
mode of communication as physical display.” We attempted to answer this puzzle by proposing
that ritual evolved from pre-symbolic, gestural communication of cooperative intent, and remained
conserved in the hominin lineage because ritual performance facilitates reliable cooperative communication, which the ephemeral properties of symbolic communication could not supplant.
While the evidence for this evolutionary model is sparse due to the biases of the archaeological
record, we hazarded a reconstruction of the evolutionary pathways of ritual behavior. Identifying
the likely onset of the key neurocognitive structures that underlie ritualized cooperative signals
and determining the primary selective pressures that would force adopting these neurocognitive
structures for cooperative communication allowed us to estimate the possible evolutionary pathways of ritualized signals. Instead of choosing one selective pressure that would push the evolution
of collective ritual, our model differs from previous accounts (Rossano, 2009; Sosis & Alcorta, 2003;
Sterelny, 2017; Turner et al., 2017) because we carved the evolutionary history of collective ritual
out from early dyadic signals affected by multiple selective pressures. Only later would these signals
interact in feedback loops with advancing cognitive and social-organization mechanisms, leading to
the complex signaling system of collective ritual we observe today.
Admittedly, while based on up-to-date, pertinent evidence [6], the nature of this reconstruction
is inherently speculative. As illustrated in Figure 1, there is ample uncertainty around the dating
of the onset of individual selective pressures, mechanisms, and signals. Our main goal was to
stimulate new research questions to enrich our understanding of collective ritual and its

ultimate causes. To further progress this investigation, we call for development in the following

areas.
The first area relates to understanding the selective pressures on cooperative signals. The pioneering work of Sosis et al. (2007) showed that while collective ritual is omnipresent across the ethnographic record, the intensity and frequency of ritual performance co-vary with socio-ecological
pressures. However, by relying on the Probability Sample File, their sample of cultural groups was
necessarily limited. A phylogenetic analysis using well-calibrated trees for thousands of ethnolinguistic groups would provide a more robust test of the importance of various selective pressures
(Šaffa et al., 2022). Likewise, combining new archaeological findings and progress in the genomic
analysis might provide better-calibrated estimates of past hominin group sizes, which would
pressure reliable cooperative communication.
Second, a more detailed understanding of neurocognitive mechanisms underlying the various
ritual signals would help precisely estimate the possible presence of individual signals in the hominin evolutionary history. This is especially true for commitment signals and their often costly
nature (Bulbulia, 2014), which rely on human psychological peculiarity (Sosis, 2003). Investigating
the ontogeny of commitment, decisions about signaling modality, and signaler psychology should
provide important insights into the functionality of commitment signals (Lang et al., 2022).
Finally, we aimed to keep as close as possible to the African archaeological record to provide a
feasible transmission model for individual cooperative signals and their underlying neurocognitive
mechanisms. This approach provides rather conservative estimates because earlier evidence for
signals/mechanisms could usually be found outside of Africa, suggesting either convergent evolution of these traits, the dependency of expression of these traits on particular ecologies, or biased
archaeological record. Nevertheless, limiting our analysis to the African continent still presents a
relatively coarse-grained approach. It fails to consider the varied distribution of past hominins and
their ecologies across the continent. While we wagered that collective ritual might have been a
regular part of the early H. sapiens’ social toolkit, the appearance and disappearance of the individual cooperative signals and their underlying neurocognitive mechanisms likely varied in African hominins pre-dating H. sapiens. Fine-grained data on partial hominin groups and their
specific ecologies would make the proposed model of the evolution of collective ritual more precise. Together, these developments might lead to further exciting advances in understanding the
most basic human social act.
