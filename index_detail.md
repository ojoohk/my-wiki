# Concept / Theory / Method Index

Alphabetical. Each entry links to the reference(s) that introduce it.

---

## B

- **Big Gods** — Norenzayan et al.'s (2016) rhetorical label for powerful, all-knowing, morally concerned supernatural agents believed to monitor social interactions and reward/sanction behavior in group-beneficial ways; treated not as a discrete type but as one corner of a multidimensional continuum of supernatural agents; rare (<10% of smallest-scale societies) but widespread in large-scale societies; by outsourcing monitoring and punishment they reduce monitoring costs and facilitate large-scale cooperation → [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **behavioral cascades** (reputation) — emergent dynamic in which one person following a Source's advice makes that Source more authoritative in others' eyes, who are then more likely to follow, providing further evidence for yet more observers; initial small differences in GS quality are amplified rather than equalized; predicts specialization without assuming initial categorical differences between specialists and laypeople → [Boyer (2021) *HN*](references/Boyer_2021_HN.md)

## C

- **causal illusion** — the tendency to infer a causal relation between two variables that may not genuinely covary; driven by attentional bias (overweighting Yes-Yes cases) and related heuristics; invoked to explain why people connect altered states of consciousness (dreams, hallucinations) to real-world misfortune, and why shamanistic interventions accumulate apparent therapeutic credibility → [Planer & Sterelny (2026) *EHB*](references/Planer_Sterelny_2026_EHB.md)
- **coalitional signals** — ritualized gestures or markings indicating membership in a specific cooperative ensemble or joint action; require shared intentionality and recursion → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md)
- **commitment signals** — costly self-inflicted behaviors signaling graded obligation to a risky joint action; honest via costly signaling mechanism → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md)
- **complex signaling systems** (ritual as) — ritual signals form a functional unit that is redundant, degenerate, and pluripotent (Barker et al., 2019; Hebets et al., 2016) → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md)
- **cooperative signaling platform** — the hypothesis that collective ritual's primary function is communicating cooperative intentions via multiple signal types → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md)
- **costly signaling theory** (in ritual/religion) — Grafen (1990) / Zahavi & Zahavi (1999) framework, whose founding statement is the handicap principle of [Zahavi (1975) *JTB*](references/Zahavi_1975_JTB.md); applied to commitment signals in ritual to explain honest signaling of cooperative intent; in Singh & Henrich (2020), operationalized as "cooperative costly signalling" (Sosis & Alcorta, 2003): observers of shamans' food/sex self-denial infer greater cooperativeness (Pr = 0.88, 95% CI: 0.78–0.94) → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md); [Power (2017) *EHB*](references/Power_2017_EHB.md); [Singh & Henrich (2020) *EHS*](references/Singh_Henrich_2020_EHS.md)
- **cultural consensus analysis** — method (Purzycki & Jamieson-Lane, 2017; AnthroTools package in R) for identifying shared cultural knowledge across respondents; applied to checklist data to identify which dietary items are consistently tabooed to shamans across cultural regions → [Singh & Henrich (2020) *EHS*](references/Singh_Henrich_2020_EHS.md)
- **credibility-enhancing displays (CREDs)** — Henrich (2009); costly acts that match one's proclaimed beliefs increase the credibility of those beliefs for observers; cited in Power (2017) as according well with the multimodal religious signaling framework; applied in Hong & Henrich (2021) to explain how observing others pay for divination inflates naive learners' efficacy estimates; first direct experimental test in shamanic context by Singh & Henrich (2020): observers infer shamans who observe taboos are stronger believers in Mentawai religious rules (Pr = 0.92, 95% CI: 0.85–0.96), the strongest of four inferences tested; in Norenzayan et al. (2016) CREDs are the proposed solution to the "Zeus Problem" and the mechanism by which extravagant displays (fasts, celibacy, painful initiations) deepen faith and spread prosocial religions → [Henrich (2009) *EHB*](references/Henrich_2009_EHB.md); [Power (2017) *EHB*](references/Power_2017_EHB.md); [Hong & Henrich (2021) *HN*](references/Hong_Henrich_2021_HN.md); [Singh & Henrich (2020) *EHS*](references/Singh_Henrich_2020_EHS.md); [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **cultural group selection (CGS)** — the process by which competition among social groups stabilized at different culturally evolved equilibria causes the spread of group-beneficial beliefs, practices, and norms; can operate through military/economic defeat of less cooperative groups, demographic competition (more culture bearers), or prestige-biased imitation of more successful groups' institutions; Henrich (2009) identifies three forms and argues CGS favors ritual–belief combinations that increase in-group cooperation; in Norenzayan et al. (2016) CGS over the last ~12 millennia is the central engine selecting prosocial-religious packages, operating through differential commune survival, conversion, fertility, and economic success; distinct from genetic group selection → [Henrich (2009) *EHB*](references/Henrich_2009_EHB.md); [Hong & Henrich (2021) *HN*](references/Hong_Henrich_2021_HN.md); [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)

## D

- **doctrinal mode of ritual** — Whitehouse's (2004) category of high-frequency, low-arousal, highly repetitious, obligatory rituals; in Norenzayan et al. (2016), cultural evolution is argued to increasingly favor this mode because repetitious rituals cue norm psychology and raise the transmission fidelity of standardized religious ideas across large, anonymous populations; Atkinson & Whitehouse (2011, HRAF) found doctrinal rituals associated with belief in Big Gods, agriculture, and societal complexity → [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **drama of strangeness** — Singh's (2018) hypothesis that trance is not a universal altered neurophysiological state but a culturally selected performance of behavioral violations of folk intuitions of humanness; convinces observers that the practitioner can interact with invisible forces; predicts that (1) trance includes non-behavioral superhuman displays, (2) humanlike trance is judged inauthentic, and (3) trance loses legitimacy when naturalized → [Singh (2018) *BBS*](references/Singh_2018_BBS.md)
- **divination as epistemic technology** — the argument that divination practices share the defining features of genuine information-seeking technologies: used for important decisions; costly and specialist-dependent; approached through repetition, result verification, and discrimination of diviners by skill — all of which only make sense if actors seek accurate information; cross-cultural ubiquity explained by systematic overestimation of efficacy via cultural transmission biases, not by non-instrumental (political/psychological) functions alone → [Hong & Henrich (2021) *HN*](references/Hong_Henrich_2021_HN.md)

## A

- **agential view of misfortune** — the widespread tendency in small-scale, pre-industrial societies to attribute serious misfortune (especially sudden death or severe illness) to other humans' occult activities; puzzling because of its high economic and social costs; coined in Planer & Sterelny (2024 *HN*), developed in full here → [Planer & Sterelny (2026) *EHB*](references/Planer_Sterelny_2026_EHB.md) — the widespread tendency in small-scale, pre-industrial societies to attribute serious misfortune (especially sudden death or severe illness) to other humans' occult activities; puzzling because of its high economic and social costs; coined in Planer & Sterelny (2024 *HN*), developed in full here → [Planer & Sterelny (2026) *EHB*](references/Planer_Sterelny_2026_EHB.md)
- **Axial Age** (critique of) — Jaspers's (1953) proposed 800–200 BCE period marking the birth of "genuine" public morality and a sharp break from earlier amoral religions (echoed by Baumard & Boyer 2013); Norenzayan et al. (2016) reject the framing on three grounds — it ignores the gradual nature of cultural evolution (pre-Axial chiefdoms/states already had partly moralizing gods), the period is too long and the transition occurs at very different times globally (Islam begins ~800 years after its close), and "pre-Axial" religions (Egyptian Maat, Hammurabi's code) already supported public morality → [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **Alexander's theory of morality** — between-group competition drives increases in society size; moral rules suppress internal fission in large groups; moralizing gods serve as impartial, immortal authority for moral norms (Alexander, 1987) → [Roes & Raymond (2003) *EHB*](references/Roes_Raymond_2003_EHB.md)

## E

- **epistemic vigilance** — the suite of cognitive capacities geared to maximizing the benefits from useful information and protecting the mind against deception (Sperber et al., 2010; Mercier & Sperber, 2017); includes automatic activation when information is received, evaluation of source quality as a stable trait, and inferences from source reputation to content validity; Boyer (2021) argues the interaction of epistemic vigilance with threat-detection psychology generates the Good Source model → [Boyer (2021) *HN*](references/Boyer_2021_HN.md); [Hong & Henrich (2021) *HN*](references/Hong_Henrich_2021_HN.md)
- **error management** (threat precaution) — when cost of missing a real threat (false negative) greatly exceeds cost of a false alarm (false positive), agents over-detect and take precautionary action despite low prior probability; explains why people follow low-plausibility religious prescriptions when the potential harm is framed as severe; derived from Haselton & Buss (2000) and applied to religious precautions → [Boyer (2021) *HN*](references/Boyer_2021_HN.md)
- **epistemic tragedy of the commons** (sorcery belief) — once the agential view of misfortune is entrenched, all would benefit from collective skepticism, but an individual turning skeptic engages in unilateral disarmament: skepticism abandons a potential accusatory weapon while not granting immunity from accusation → [Planer & Sterelny (2026) *EHB*](references/Planer_Sterelny_2026_EHB.md)
- **erosion of mutualism model** — cultural evolutionary process (five conditions): (i) socially salient subgroup controls intergenerational lore; (ii) initial lore is mutually beneficial; (iii) subgroup interests diverge from community's; (iv) subgroup can influence community practices to their advantage; (v) myside bias cumulatively drifts lore toward minority benefit; trust infrastructure established while lore is still beneficial makes later drift hard to resist → [Planer & Sterelny (2026) *EHB*](references/Planer_Sterelny_2026_EHB.md)
- **extended phenotype (cultural)** — Dawkins concept extended to cultural production; cultural products are manifestations of producer gene expression in other minds; cognitive faculties used in cultural creation (theory of mind, language) are under selection because they serve producer fitness → [André et al. (2023) *EHS*](references/Andre_etal_2023_EHS.md)

## P

- **putt'a / p'ulda (attaching / unbinding metaphors)** — two versatile, valence-neutral metaphors Walraven (2002) identifies as underlying many actions in Korean *kut*: *putt'a* (touching, attaching) transfers blessings by physical contact, while *p'ulda* (taking away, unbinding, dissolving) removes frustration, illness, or ancestral dissatisfaction; deftly combined into ritual sequences (e.g., *ko p'uri*, "undoing the knots") → [Walraven (2002) *RKS*](references/Walraven_2002_RKS.md)
- **prosocial religions** — Norenzayan et al.'s (2016) term for the culturally evolved packages combining Big Gods, credible displays of faith (CREDs), solidarity-building rituals, pronatalism, fictive kinship, in-group markers, and sacred values; defined as a statistical pattern governed by specific hypotheses rather than a concept with necessary/sufficient features; argued to have spread over the last 10–12 millennia by promoting parochial cooperation and reproductive/economic success in intergroup competition → [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **prestige-biased transmission** — Henrich & Gil-White (2001); learners preferentially pay attention to and learn from others based on cues of prestige, success, skill, age, ethnicity (dialect, dress), and sex; operates across domains of expertise; in the context of CREDs, prestigious individuals who perform CREDs have disproportionate influence on learners' belief commitment; high-prestige individuals also have stronger incentives to make self-serving claims, so the CRED adaptation is calibrated to discount prestigious sources more when they advocate for their own interests → [Henrich (2009) *EHB*](references/Henrich_2009_EHB.md)
- **prior beliefs (Bayesian cultural transmission)** — in cultural evolutionary models of belief formation, priors (α₀, β₀ in beta distribution) represent intuitive or culturally transmitted expectations about a practice's efficacy; because each new generation "reasserts" fresh priors before updating, strong priors prevent empirical correction from converging beliefs to true efficacy even across many generations → [Hong & Henrich (2021) *HN*](references/Hong_Henrich_2021_HN.md)
- **producers' standpoint** (cultural evolution) — argument that fitness costs and benefits of cultural *production* should be the primary analytical focus in cultural evolution models, rather than consumer/recipient properties; addresses why production costs are incurred in the first place → [André et al. (2023) *EHS*](references/Andre_etal_2023_EHS.md)

## U

- **uncertain outcomes** — Singh's (2018) category for events that are simultaneously (1) uncontrollable by the individual, (2) fitness-relevant, and (3) random (outcomes cannot be predicted with available knowledge); these three conditions together maximally invite superstitious thinking and thus constitute the core jurisdiction of shamans; distinguished from controllable outcomes, unimportant uncontrollable outcomes, and outcomes that never occur → [Singh (2018) *BBS*](references/Singh_2018_BBS.md)
- **underreporting of negative evidence** (epistemic technologies) — mechanism sustaining false beliefs in ineffective practices: confirmation bias causes failure to notice disconfirming cases; practitioners have reputational incentives to advertise successes; norm psychology makes revealing failures a norm violation in communities where the practice is dominant; cumulatively shifts population-level beliefs above true efficacy even under Bayesian updating → [Hong & Henrich (2021) *HN*](references/Hong_Henrich_2021_HN.md)

## S

- **shiwon-hada (kut as psychological transaction)** — the recurrent emic criterion of an effective Korean shamanic ritual for both shamans and clients (Walraven 2002): "to feel relieved" (from oppression, sorrow), such that the client is liberated from worries and given confidence to face the future, even when the ostensible aim (pregnancy, business success, passing an exam) is not itself secured; antonym *taptap-hada* → [Walraven (2002) *RKS*](references/Walraven_2002_RKS.md)
- **supernatural punishment hypothesis (SPH)** — adaptationist account (Bering 2006; 2011; Johnson 2009) that fear of a moralizing, punishing god is a genetic adaptation favored by within-group selection to restrain individual defection; Norenzayan et al. (2016) treat it as compatible in principle but raise two challenges from the ethnographic record — forager gods have limited omniscience and little moral concern, and gods become *more* moralizing as societies scale up and anonymity makes escaping social sanctions easier — and instead argue supernatural punishment beliefs were culturally rather than genetically selected → [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **supernatural monitoring (third-party)** — the hypothesis that humans' evolved sensitivity to cues of social surveillance (e.g., watching-eyes effects) is exploited by belief in morally concerned supernatural watchers with greater surveillance capacity and power to punish, expanding norm compliance beyond that produced by human watchers and earthly sanctions; central to the prosocial-religions account of how cooperation scales among strangers → [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **supernatural otherness** (hypothesis) — Singh & Henrich (2020); the proposal that observers infer someone who self-denies to be fundamentally different from normal humans, and that this perceived difference makes it more conceivable that the self-denier possesses superhuman powers (healing, divine contact); operationally predicts that observers of shamans' taboo adherence infer (a) greater dissimilarity from normal humans and (b) greater supernatural power; empirically supported among Mentawai participants (difference: Pr = 0.78, 95% CI: 0.64–0.88; power: Pr = 0.84, 95% CI: 0.72–0.91); proposed mechanisms (unspecified by prior theorists) include strange preferences, unique cognitive abilities, or transformation through denial → [Singh & Henrich (2020) *EHS*](references/Singh_Henrich_2020_EHS.md)
- **specialist essentialism** — the cross-cultural tendency to construe religious specialists (shamans, healers, diviners) not as technically trained but as possessing stable, inherent, often inherited internal qualities that make them essentially different from ordinary people; Boyer (2021) explains this as an emergent folk-sociological response to reputation cascades that participants cannot perceive at the aggregate level; documented cross-culturally in Winkelman's (1986, 1990) HRAF-based surveys → [Boyer (2021) *HN*](references/Boyer_2021_HN.md)
- **shamanism (Singh 2018 definition)** — practitioners who enter trance to provide services; deliberately broad to enable cross-cultural analysis; encompasses medicine men, mediums, and prophets; contrasts with narrower Siberian-specific or soul-journey-only definitions → [Singh (2018) *BBS*](references/Singh_2018_BBS.md)
- **shamanism (Winkelman 2012 definition)** — cluster of practices in which: (i) altered states of consciousness play a fundamental role; (ii) practitioners interact with the spirit world by travelling through it; (iii) shamans make such journeys on behalf of others; (iv) healing and divination are most central; (v) spirit travel is surrounded by group-defining emotionally powerful rituals; (vi) shamans exercise agency and control; (vii) laity may also have mind-altering experiences but shaman guides and interprets; widely distributed across forager communities → [Planer & Sterelny (2026) *EHB*](references/Planer_Sterelny_2026_EHB.md)
- **social evolution theory** (applied to cultural production) — Hamilton (1964) four-type matrix (altruism, selfishness, mutualism, spite) applied to classify cultural production by joint producer/consumer fitness effects; predicts altruistic production concentrates on kin, selfish production requires manipulation, mutualistic production dominates when interests align → [André et al. (2023) *EHS*](references/Andre_etal_2023_EHS.md)
- **symbolic culture** — operational domain including art, narratives, religious representations, games, sports, ethnic ideologies, moral norms, and social conventions; defined by apparent difficulty of explaining in terms of direct fitness advantages → [André et al. (2023) *EHS*](references/Andre_etal_2023_EHS.md)

## H

- **handicap principle** — Zahavi's (1975) proposal that characters evolving through mate preference necessarily confer a survival handicap on the selected sex, and that the handicap is *the mechanism of the signal*, not a by-product of runaway selection (contra Fisher 1930; O'Donald 1962): because the organism was already at equilibrium with natural selection, adding a preference pressure with no survival value must have a negative effect, so character magnitude is correlated with the handicap imposed; an individual bearing a well-developed character has thereby survived a test, and the more developed the character, the more severe the test; crucially requires **no genetic linkage** between marker and quality — since any such linkage is open to bluff (low-quality individuals tagged with a high-quality marker), the cost of the handicap is what excludes bluff; Zahavi refines it to predict the handicap falls on a character important to the selecting sex and tied to the species' ecological problems; offered as an individual-level alternative to group selection (contra Wynne-Edwards 1962 on territory size) and to reciprocal altruism (contra Trivers 1971 on sea bird "altruistic" plumage), neither of which act directly on the individual → [Zahavi (1975) *JTB*](references/Zahavi_1975_JTB.md)
- **hurdle model** — two-part statistical model for count data with excess zeros; binomial component predicts if outcome is positive (any nomination); truncated negative binomial component predicts magnitude among positive cases; used to model skewed reputational nomination counts → [Power (2017) *EHB*](references/Power_2017_EHB.md)

## C

- **Carneiro's circumscription theory** — resource-rich environments concentrate intergroup competition, driving larger polities; first statistical test in Roes & Raymond (2003) → [Roes & Raymond (2003) *EHB*](references/Roes_Raymond_2003_EHB.md)

## E

- **Ethnographic Atlas (EA)** — Murdock (1967) cross-cultural database; 862 (1967) / 1267 (1990/1999) preindustrial societies; used for cross-cultural hypothesis testing → [Roes & Raymond (2003) *EHB*](references/Roes_Raymond_2003_EHB.md)

## F

- **fireside niche** — the extension of human wakefulness by fire control (Dunbar & Gowlett, 2014); fire delayed sleep onset (melatonin inhibition by light); created a niche of low-opportunity-cost socializing, storytelling, song, and ritual around the campfire; proposed as the primary site for elaborating animistic philosophy and shamanistic culture → [Planer & Sterelny (2026) *EHB*](references/Planer_Sterelny_2026_EHB.md)

## G

- **Good Source model** — speculative model (Boyer, 2021) of communication dynamics in small, face-to-face ancestral communities; four hypotheses: (H1) benefit to having Good Sources in one's epistemic environment; (H2) benefit to being seen as a Good Source; (H3) probability of following a recommendation is a function of perceived source epistemic quality; (H4) perceived source quality is a function of third-party followers; predicts threat-related messages, inscrutable processes, specific diagnoses, specialist role concentration, and essentialist construal of specialists; the model is productive-side: explains why people *formulate* threat explanations, not just why they *accept* them → [Boyer (2021) *HN*](references/Boyer_2021_HN.md)
- **Galton's problem** — statistical non-independence of societies in cross-cultural samples due to cultural diffusion; the SCCS was designed to reduce it → [Roes & Raymond (2003) *EHB*](references/Roes_Raymond_2003_EHB.md)

## K

- **kongsu (oracles)** — in Korean *kut*, speech in which deities and spirits address clients directly through the shaman's mouth; the core of every ritual (Walraven 2002, drawing on Bruno 1998/2001 and Guillemoz 1994); not one-way but a two-directional flow that lets the shaman learn and further personalize the ritual; many recurrent phrases are "performative" (reassurances, promises, pledges whose utterance is itself an act), and the nimble alternation of deity and shaman voices — with Korean sentence mood surfacing only at the end — obscures the difference between a shaman's wish and a deity's promise → [Walraven (2002) *RKS*](references/Walraven_2002_RKS.md)

## J

- **jurisdictional hierarchy** — EA variable 33 / SCCS variable 237; number of levels of political authority beyond the local community (1–5); standard proxy for society size in cross-cultural research → [Roes & Raymond (2003) *EHB*](references/Roes_Raymond_2003_EHB.md)

## M

- **myside bias** (cultural transmission) — the tendency to produce arguments or evidence in support of preexisting beliefs; documented in cognitive psychology; operates unconsciously; when socially salient agents transmit lore, successive iterations of this bias cumulatively drift the lore toward minority benefit without any individual aware of the drift → [Planer & Sterelny (2026) *EHB*](references/Planer_Sterelny_2026_EHB.md)
- **moralizing gods** — supernatural beings who are active in human affairs and specifically supportive of human morality (EA variable 34 / SCCS variable 238); predicted to be more prevalent in larger societies under Alexander's theory; Norenzayan et al. (2016) review the same cross-cultural finding (moralizing gods rare in small-scale societies, common in large-scale ones) and treat its modern prevalence as an anthropological puzzle requiring a cultural-evolutionary, not innate-cognitive, explanation → [Roes & Raymond (2003) *EHB*](references/Roes_Raymond_2003_EHB.md); [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **multimodal signals** (religious practice) — different modes of religious practice (regular worship, public ritual acts, possession) operating as distinct signaling channels with distinct costs, observability, and reputational implications; framework from behavioral ecology applied to religion → [Power (2017) *EHB*](references/Power_2017_EHB.md)
- **multiplex signals** (religious practice) — each religious modality simultaneously conveying information about multiple distinct character traits; different receivers may attend to different aspects of the same signal (e.g., strength vs. devotion from the same ritual act) → [Power (2017) *EHB*](references/Power_2017_EHB.md)
- **mutualistic cooperation** — cooperative arrangement where both parties benefit from the joint action; primary type facilitated by ritual signaling → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md)

## O

- **ontogenetic ritualization** — process by which dyads shorten idiosyncratic gesture sequences into simple placeholders through repeated interaction (Tomasello et al., 1997) → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md)

## R

- **rational actor axiom (applied to religion)** — the assumption, drawn from Stark & Finke's *Acts of Faith* (2000), that believers do not act arbitrarily but make calculated choices aimed at maximal (material or spiritual) returns; Walraven (2002) extends it to less-organized, non-Western religion, treating clients of Korean shamans as having good reasons to commission *kut* and studying ritual *practice* rather than reified tradition → [Walraven (2002) *RKS*](references/Walraven_2002_RKS.md)
- **religious priming** — experimental paradigm in which religious concepts are activated (consciously or implicitly) before measuring behavior; Norenzayan et al. (2016) report a meta-analysis (Shariff et al.) of 25 studies / 4,825 participants showing religious reminders reliably increase prosocial behavior (Hedges' *g* = 0.27, 95% CI 0.15–0.40), robust to publication-bias adjustment; a broader 93-study / 11,653-participant analysis found the effect moderated by prior belief — reliable for believers, absent for nonbelievers → [Norenzayan et al. (2016) *BBS*](references/Norenzayan_etal_2016_BBS.md)
- **religions in the wild** — Boyer's (2021) term for religious activities that occurred before organized, doctrinal religions or persist at their margins; characterized by focus on particular cases of misfortune, explanation via superhuman agents, and specialist services; includes shamanism, ancestor cults, anti-witchcraft activities, divination, protection against mystical harm; contrasted with doctrinal religions on six dimensions (no central doctrine, individual practitioners, particular-case focus, no unified services, local gods, no membership) → [Boyer (2021) *HN*](references/Boyer_2021_HN.md)
- **reputation (religious signaling)** — reputational qualities attributed to individuals by peers based on observed religious practice; operationalized as free-list nominations for character traits; central outcome variable in Power (2017) → [Power (2017) *EHB*](references/Power_2017_EHB.md)
- **ritual (Rappaport definition)** — "the performance of more or less invariant sequences of formal acts and utterances not entirely encoded by the performers" (Rappaport, 1999, p. 24) → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md)

## S

- **sexual selection (Zahavi's mate-preference model)** — the reframing of Darwinian sexual selection as a quality-detection system: sexual selection is effective because it improves the ability of the selecting sex to detect quality in the selected sex; both sexes gain (assurance of mate quality; better advertisement) and both lose (males invest time/energy/risk in advertising; females get less parental help and bear sons less fit under natural selection); females *compromise*, taking a handicapped male because an unhandicapped high-quality male cannot advertise and so cannot be spotted; proceeds only while the assurance gained exceeds the cost of the handicap and while males can survive it; where parental-investment pressures are too tight for a handicap, mate selection is less reliable and takes longer (cf. Lein 1973 on monomorphic vs. dimorphic warblers) → [Zahavi (1975) *JTB*](references/Zahavi_1975_JTB.md)
- **signaling theory of religion** — hypothesis that costly religious acts honestly signal commitment to the prosocial tenets of the religious community; enables cooperative communities to resist free-riders; Power (2017) extends to test whether receivers discern these signals → [Power (2017) *EHB*](references/Power_2017_EHB.md)
- **similarity signals** — ritualized mimicry or synchronous movement indicating shared interests / mutual relatedness; earliest of three ritual signal types (~1.76 Ma H. ergaster) → [Lang & Kundt (2023) *RBB*](references/Lang_Kundt_2023_RBB.md)
- **Standard Cross-Cultural Sample (SCCS)** — 186 societies selected from the EA to represent independent cultural types; reduces Galton's problem; >1800 variables; used for cross-cultural hypothesis testing → [Roes & Raymond (2003) *EHB*](references/Roes_Raymond_2003_EHB.md)

## T

- **transformative practices** (shamanism) — Singh's (2018) umbrella term for the initiation rites, ascetic observances, and peculiarities (epilepsy, extra fingers, ambiguous sexual identity) that convince community members a practitioner has biologically or psychologically transformed into a non-human entity capable of interacting with invisible forces; explicitly distinguished from cooperative costly signals on four grounds; create the entry requirements that professionalize shamanism → [Singh (2018) *BBS*](references/Singh_2018_BBS.md)
- **threat-detection psychology** — the set of evolved cognitive responses to direct and potential fitness threats (Boyer & Bergstrom, 2011); includes prepared fears, conditioning, and verbal communication as the main route to learning about threat in humans; characterized by negativity bias (preferential attention to negative/threat information), loss aversion, risk-sensitivity under high-cost conditions, and error-management logic (over-detect when miss cost >> false-alarm cost); Boyer (2021) argues this system jointly with epistemic vigilance produces the Good Source model dynamics explaining wild religious traditions → [Boyer (2021) *HN*](references/Boyer_2021_HN.md)
