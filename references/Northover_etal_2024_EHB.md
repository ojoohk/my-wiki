# Northover, Quillien, Conroy-Beam, and Cohen (2024) Religious Signaling and Prosociality: A Review of the Literature

#type/paper #theme/HBE #theme/Cooperation #journal/EHB #theory/costly_signaling_theory_religion

**Bibliography**: Northover, Stefanie B., Tadeg Quillien, Daniel Conroy-Beam, and Adam B. Cohen. 2024. "Religious Signaling and Prosociality: A Review of the Literature." *Evolution and Human Behavior* 45:106593.

## Topic

This review examines whether costly religious behaviors, badges, and bans ("religious practice") increase trust and prosociality, as predicted by the costly signaling theory of religion (CST-R). The authors review empirical studies published by early 2023 and organize them around two questions: (1) does religious practice enhance trust and prosociality? (2) does religious practice signal ingroup commitment? The review is motivated by inconsistencies in the empirical literature and aims to map what is and is not established.

## Key Theory / Framework

The costly signaling theory of religion, dubbed by Sosis and Bressler (2003), proposes that costly religious behaviors, badges, and bans are credible signals of commitment to the ingroup and its moral code. The three signal categories are: *behaviors* (ritual performance, service attendance, tithing, prayer, pilgrimage — costing time, energy, money); *badges* (physical markers of group membership, such as headscarves, crosses, forehead markings — costing potential stigma, physical discomfort, or reduced attractiveness); and *bans* (food taboos, technology restrictions, sexual prohibitions — costing access to beneficial resources).

Costly signals are proposed to solve the free-rider problem: committed members signal by accepting costs that uncommitted members would not bear, thereby enabling ingroup members to identify reliable cooperators. The authors describe two honesty mechanisms. First, differential costs and benefits: opportunity costs of compliance are greater for outsiders and secularly successful individuals than for committed believers (Iannaccone, 1992; Carr & Landa, 1983). Second, differential anticipated costs and benefits: true believers anticipate supernatural rewards and punishments that alter their cost-benefit calculus in ways skeptics do not share (Bulbulia, 2004b; Sosis, 2003).

The authors propose that religious signals are probably the result of interplay between biological and cultural evolution. Evolved cognitive mechanisms for assessing social contracts, coalition commitment, and trustworthiness (Cosmides, Barrett, & Tooby, 2010; Tooby & Cosmides, 2010) provide the infrastructure on which cultural evolution builds. Cultural practices involving costly signals become successful cultural attractors because individuals are predisposed to view costly signals as more reliable (Henrich, 2009).

## Review Scope & Method

This is a narrative review article with no original data collection. The authors conducted a PsycInfo search on January 8, 2023, using the terms "relig*" AND "signal*." They identified additional papers from citation searches. Inclusion criteria: studies measuring religious practice together with trust, prosociality, or perceptions of trustworthiness or prosociality of religious actors. Studies focusing solely on religious identity (not practice) were excluded. Effect sizes are reported or calculated for each finding reviewed.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Religious actors are not consistently more prosocial in behavioral economic games

Evidence for actual prosociality is mixed. Xygalatas et al. (2013), studying the Mauritian Hindu festival of Thaipusam, found that kavadi performers and observers donated more to a temple than low-ordeal ritual participants (Cohen's *d* = 0.73); among those who performed or observed the kavadi, greater perceived pain predicted greater donations (*r* = 0.36). However, Bulbulia and Mahoney (2008) found that hours in Christian practice among New Zealand Christians was not significantly correlated with generosity toward other Christians (*r* = 0.19). Xygalatas et al. (2018) studied Hindu Mauritians in a Random Allocation Game; kavadi frequency was associated with fairer allocations (β = −0.09), but personal prayer (β = 0.02) and pilgrimage frequency (β = −0.04) were not significant. Atkinson (2018) on Tanna, Vanuatu, found prayer frequency predicted less cheating (OR = 1.59) but church attendance did not (statistics unreported). Soler (2012) found that Candomblé Religious Signaling Scale (CRSS) scores predicted self-reported cooperative acts (β = 0.31, *n* = 206) and public goods game contributions (β = 0.22, *n* = 199) with fellow *terreiro* members. Sosis and Ruffle (2003, 2004) found that among religious men in Israeli kibbutzim, synagogue attendance was negatively correlated with shekels claimed in a common-pool resource game (*r* = −0.18), driven by daily attenders; no such relationship emerged for religious women (*r* = 0.05) or secular kibbutz members. The authors characterize the overall evidence as inconclusive.

### Finding 2: Religious actors are broadly perceived as especially trustworthy by ingroup members; evidence from outgroup members is mixed

The strongest and most consistent evidence in the review concerns perceived trustworthiness. Ruffle and Sosis (2010) found that Israeli Jews in the praying condition rated wallet return by strangers more likely than those in music (Cohen's *d* = 0.84) and workout (Cohen's *d* = 0.39) conditions. Purzycki and Arakchaa (2013) found that Tyvans judged fictional characters who left cairn offerings more trustworthy (*r* = 0.24). Hall et al. (2015) showed that Christian undergraduates rated targets as more trustworthy when they gave 10% of income to a religious charity (partial η² = 0.08 and 0.03 in two experiments) and when they obeyed dietary bans (partial η² = 0.03); a conjunction fallacy task further demonstrated strong associative links between trustworthiness and adherence (*r* = 0.73 for untrustworthy actors assumed to be non-adherent). McCullough et al. (2016) found that faces bearing Christian badges (Ash Wednesday cross, cross necklace) were rated more trustworthy by both Christian and non-Christian participants. Power (2017a) in a 20-month field study of two South Indian villages (N = 782, 97% of population) found that regular worshippers (≥once/week) were more likely to be nominated as generous (OR = 1.83); public ritual frequency weighted by cost also predicted generosity nominations (OR = 1.04), but possession rituals did not (OR = 0.62). Singh and Henrich (2020) found that 68 Mentawai villagers judged a shaman who abstained from non-required resources more generous and trustworthy than a minimally compliant shaman (mean estimated probability of choosing extra-abstaining shaman *Pr* = 0.88).

In contrast, Shaver, Lang, et al. (2018) in Mauritius found that ingroup ancestry targets with ingroup badges were rated nonsignificantly more trustworthy, while ingroup ancestry targets with outgroup badges were rated significantly *less* trustworthy. Chia and Jih (1994) found that Illinois Christian high schoolers associated positive traits with Catholic-attired targets more often, whereas Malaysian Muslim high schoolers associated positive traits with those targets *less* often (no standardized effect sizes available).

### Finding 3: Religious actors receive more trust and resources from ingroup members; outgroup evidence is mixed

McCullough et al. (2016) found that Christianly badged faces received more money in a trust game from both Christian and non-Christian participants. Shaver, Lang, et al. (2018) found that ingroup ancestry targets bearing ingroup badges were significantly more likely to receive money in a trust investment game (OR = 3.96) and received greater amounts; outgroup badges had no significant effect on target ancestry targets (OR = 1.16 ingroup badge; OR = 1.21 outgroup badge). Blais et al. (2018) found no behavioral difference in round-one trust decisions toward practicing versus non-practicing partners (45 vs. 43 decisions, partial η² = 0.01), though parietal alpha suppression (a neural correlate of trust-readiness) was positively correlated with trust decisions for participants with practicing partners but negatively correlated for those with non-practicing partners. Chen (2010), analyzing Indonesian household data from May 1997 to August 1999, found that households that increased Qur'an study group attendance during the financial crisis were 49% less likely to be credit constrained four months later; households with unchanged attendance were 23% less likely; decreased attendance 21% less likely; those without attendance 6% less likely. Ruffle and Sosis (2010) found that participants in the praying condition indicated greater (hypothetical) altruism than those in music (Cohen's *d* = 0.48) and workout (Cohen's *d* = 0.55) conditions.

### Finding 4: Evidence for intragroup cooperation within practicing groups is sparse and inconsistent

Sosis (2000) analyzed 200 American communes: religious communes lasted a mean of 25.3 years versus 6.4 years for secular communes, and within the first two years, secular communes were four times as likely to dissolve as religious communes. [Sosis and Bressler (2003)](../references/Sosis_2003_HN.md) found a positive correlation between number of costly requirements and commune longevity for religious communes but not secular communes; however, the number of costly requirements did not predict failure due to economic troubles or internal dispute even among religious communes. Sosis et al. (2007) in 60 societies found no relationship between costliness of male rites and cooperative labor, food sharing, or cooperation as a social value, but did find costlier rites in societies with more frequent external and internal warfare. The authors describe this evidence as insufficient to draw conclusions.

### Finding 5: Routine religious behavior is associated with ingroup favoritism; high-cost collective rituals are associated with expanded social identity and outgroup tolerance

Orbell et al. (1992) found that church attendance positively predicted cooperation in a prisoner's dilemma for Mormons in Logan, Utah (*r* = 0.27, 0.15, 0.16 across three measures), but not for non-Mormons in Logan (*r* = −0.02, −0.10, 0.00) nor for participants in Eugene-Springfield, Oregon. Shaver, Lang, et al. (2018) found that higher frequency of temple/church attendance predicted sending more money to ingroup ancestry targets with ingroup badges and less money to outgroup ancestry targets with outgroup badges. In contrast, Xygalatas et al. (2013) found that kavadi performers and observers identified more broadly (toward "Mauritian" rather than "Hindu") after the high-ordeal ritual. Clingingsmith et al. (2009) used a Pakistani government Hajj lottery (*n* = 1,605 applicants) as a natural experiment; Hajjis reported less parochial Muslim practice and more universal practice five to eight months after the Hajj, and were more likely to say non-Muslims were equal to Muslims. Alnabulsi et al. (2020) surveyed 1,176 pilgrims from 72 countries during the Hajj; increased Muslim identification and increased positive outgroup attitudes both resulted from the same process—perceptions of the crowd as cooperative and united—mediated by crowd identification. The authors note that routine (low-cost, frequent, smaller-scale) religious practice and high-cost infrequent collective practice appear to produce different identity and prosociality effects, with the former linked to ingroup favoritism and the latter to expanded identity and outgroup tolerance.

### Finding 6: Secular costly behaviors do not consistently increase intragroup cooperation in secular groups but can increase perceived trustworthiness of individuals

Shaver, DiVietro, et al. (2018) studied 11 US college groups (4 fraternities, 4 clubs, 3 intro classes); fraternities imposed greater costs than clubs and fraternity trustees returned more money (more trustworthy), but within fraternities, pledge costs were negatively related to self-reported trust. [Sosis and Bressler (2003)](../references/Sosis_2003_HN.md) similarly found costly requirements predicted longevity for religious but not secular communes. On the other hand, Ellis et al. (2018) found that MTurk workers rated a secular vegetarian target as more trustworthy when he abstained from meat, and the effect was larger in the secular (vegetarian) context than in the religious (Muslim) context. Northover and Cohen (2017b) found that following versus breaking a moral dietary rule increased trustworthiness ratings (partial η² = 0.11) regardless of whether the rule was framed as secular or religious; the interaction term was nonsignificant (partial η² = 0.00).

### Finding 7: Integrity mediates the effect of religious practice on perceived trustworthiness

Ellis et al. (2018) found that when ability, benevolence, and integrity were included in statistical models, the effect of religious practice on trust was eliminated, consistent with integrity as a key mediator. In a direct manipulation experiment, participants assigned to a high-integrity condition rated the target as significantly more trustworthy (partial η² = 0.26), and in that context religious practice had no additional effect (partial η² = 0.00). The authors suggest that perceivers may trust religious actors because they infer that such actors consistently follow a moral code, which is the definition of integrity in Mayer, Davis, and Schoorman's (1995) model.

### Finding 8: Costlier practice does not reliably produce larger effects on perceived trustworthiness

Northover et al. (2016) found that a Muslim target who obeyed dietary rules at high personal cost (distant halal store, time-consuming, expensive) was rated only marginally more trustworthy than a target who obeyed the same rules with negligible cost (Cohen's *d* = 0.10, *p* = .72), and this was replicated (Cohen's *d* = 0.15, *p* = .41). Both were significantly more trustworthy than the disobedient target. In studies involving religious badges (cross necklaces, prayer ashes, headscarves), apparent effects emerged even though these badges were characterized by the authors as of low cost in most cases. The authors propose that perceivers may use the number and frequency of behaviors, badges, and bans as a heuristic more than total cost, and may assume that badge-wearers also perform costlier private acts.

## Relevance to This Project

*(No active project tagged.)*

This review provides the most comprehensive current inventory of empirical tests of the costly signaling theory of religion. The systematic contrast between actual prosociality (inconclusive) and perceived prosociality (robust for ingroup members) is directly useful for any project engaging with religion and cooperation. The distinction between routine practice → ingroup favoritism and high-cost collective ritual → expanded identity has implications for theories about between-group versus within-group dynamics of religion. The finding that integrity mediates the practice–trust link, and that secular behaviors can produce similar effects, raises questions about what specifically religious signaling adds beyond moral commitment more generally.
