# André, Baumard, and Boyer (2023) Cultural Evolution from the Producers' Standpoint

#type/paper #theme/CulturalEvolution #theme/Religion #journal/EHS #theory/social_evolution_theory #theory/extended_phenotype_cultural

**Bibliography**: André, Jean-Baptiste, Nicolas Baumard, and Pascal Boyer. 2023. "Cultural Evolution from the Producers' Standpoint." *Evolutionary Human Sciences* 5:e25, 1–12. https://doi.org/10.1017/ehs.2023.20

## Topic

The paper argues that standard cultural evolution models focus overwhelmingly on the recipients or consumers of cultural information, and thereby fail to explain the fitness costs incurred in producing the behaviours and artefacts that become culturally widespread. The authors propose that cultural evolution should center on the fitness costs and benefits of cultural *production*, treating cultural products as parts of the extended phenotype of producers whose interests may or may not align with those of consumers.

## Key Theory / Framework

The paper applies **social evolution theory** (Hamilton, 1964) to cultural production. Cultural production is classified as a social behaviour in which a producer acts upon a consumer; the four-type Hamilton matrix (altruism, selfishness, mutualism, spite) provides the organizing framework for classifying all instances of cultural production by their joint effect on producer and consumer direct fitness (Table 1).

The paper situates itself against two existing consumer-focused frameworks: **dual inheritance theory** (Boyd & Richerson, 1985), which emphasizes content-free heuristics such as conformist bias and prestige bias; and **cultural attraction theory** / epidemiology of culture (Claidière, Scott-Phillips, & Sperber, 2014; Sperber & Claidière, 2006), which emphasizes domain-specific evolved preferences shaping acquisition and reconstruction of cultural information. The authors argue both traditions share the assumption that cultural evolution is primarily explained by properties of receivers. Neither addresses why production costs are incurred in the first place.

The adaptationist prediction driving the paper: producers should have a motivation to generate or modify cultural items if and only if doing so will have a positive impact on their inclusive biological fitness. Cultural fitness of an item is conditioned by its effect on the biological fitness of its producer (citing El Mouden, André, Morin, & Nettle, 2014; Micheletti, 2020; Nettle, 2020).

**Symbolic culture** is defined operationally as the domain including art, narratives, religious representations, games, sports, ethnic ideologies, superstitions, moral norms, and social conventions — "the apparent difficulty in explaining a great variety of cultural phenomena in terms of practical goals or direct fitness benefits."

## Data & Methods

No original data reported. This is a theoretical and illustrative paper. The authors explicitly state: "Data availability statement. No original data are reported in this article."

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Consumer-focused models cannot explain production costs

Standard models explain *why* cultural items spread once produced (they fit evolved consumer preferences — e.g., music exploits auditory cortex properties; stories satisfy social motivation to understand others' lives). But this does not explain the large investment in production: "listening to Mozart or the Beatles is indeed riveting (on the consumption side), but that does not by itself explain the thousands of hours of training invested by composers and performers." The analytical gap is that production and consumption engage different interests; the pleasure of an audience is not a benefit to the composer or performer.

### Finding 2: Altruistic production is directed at close kin

Cultural production is altruistic when it has a negative effect on the producer's direct fitness and a positive effect on the consumer's. Social evolution theory predicts this pattern will be limited to close genetic relatives. The examples the authors give are pedagogic interaction (parents attract infant attention to generic information in universally documented infant-directed instruction) and infant-directed singing (universal across cultures; Mehr, Singh, York, Glowacki, & Krasnow, 2018). Both cases satisfy the kin-selection condition: the offspring's benefit is an indirect fitness benefit to the parent.

### Finding 3: Selfish production requires manipulation and characterizes extractive religious specialists

When production benefits the producer and costs the consumer, producers must exploit imperfect consumer filtering. The authors apply this to religious cult leaders who extract labour, money, and sexual services (Dawson, 1998). The framework also applies to shamans, diviners, and ritual specialists in small-scale societies (citing Singh, 2018; Winkelman, 1990): these specialists produce most current religious representations and offer religious goods, gaining social status, material resources, and prestige that may translate into direct fitness benefits. The authors treat this as an empirical rather than necessary classification: "the answer is case specific."

### Finding 4: Honest signaling (automatic mutualism) explains sports as cultural productions

Mutualistic cultural production with automatic benefits occurs when producer and consumer share a common interest, as in sexual selection: consumer preferences create selective pressure for specific producer traits. The authors apply this to sport, characterized as rule-bound public displays of physical qualities with a documented gender imbalance in nonmodern cultures (Wiedemann, Barton, & Hill, 2012). The constraining rules of sport convert multi-dimensional individual differences into clear rankings that serve as proxies for mate value (Miller, 1999); the authors also cite the Melanesian land dives (Pentecost island and others) as ritual ceremonies functioning analogously to courtship displays.

### Finding 5: Conditional cooperation (reciprocal mutualism) explains norms and traditional medicine

When producers do not automatically benefit but gain through consumer exchange, the fitness value of information to consumers can spill over to producers. Traditional medicine: effective cures (e.g., empirical plant knowledge) coexist with ineffective practices (bloodletting); producers gain reputational advantages from patient belief in efficacy, while consumers may receive placebo benefits (Price, Finniss, & Benedetti, 2008). Conventional norms (market conventions, children's games, highway regulations, dress codes): producers benefit from providing and maintaining coordination points that are also in the interest of consumers. Puritanical norms: the authors argue these raise the cost of sexual promiscuity, making stable pair-bonding advantageous for both sexes — reducing cuckoldry risk for men, desertion risk for women — and that they are more prevalent in societies where high parental investment strategies are advantageous (Baumard & Chevallier, 2015; Baumard, Hyafil, Morris, & Boyer, 2015).

### Finding 6: Producers' interests explain the content-features of symbolic culture

Taking the producers' standpoint generates substantive hypotheses about recurrent cultural forms that consumer models leave unexplained. Religious representations across small-scale societies focus overwhelmingly on misfortune prevention and attribution to spirits, gods, ancestors, or witches (Boyer, 2019, 2022; Strathern, 2019). Consumer models explain this via threat-detection psychology. The producers' model adds: precaution psychology creates a niche for claiming expertise without delivering valid information, because precautionary information is generally not tested once accepted. Specialists who convince others they can manage threats receive reputation and social support; the frequent use of trance is interpreted as a signal of such capacities (Singh, 2018).

Visual arts are designed to signal agency (that they exist because of a creator's intention; Gell, 1998) and to jointly signal (a) representational intention and (b) difficulty of production — creating a "sweet spot" with skillful figurative representation as a preferred genre across cultures (Dutton, 2009). In music, successful genres balance proximity to intuitive tonal expectations (signalling non-randomness) with anticipation and surprise (signalling unique skill) (Huron, 2006).

### Finding 7: Cultural products constitute the producer's extended phenotype

The paper concludes that cultural production is part of the human extended phenotype (citing Borau & Bonnefon, 2020; Sterelny, 2018): phenotype includes all manifestations of gene expression inside or outside the organism. Convincing shamanistic cures and good stories may increase their producers' fitness in the same way as architectural constructions increase the fitness of beavers or termites. Cognitive faculties involved in cultural creation (theory of mind, language) are therefore under selection. This reframes the concept of culture: culture is not "something" that acts on humans, but individuals use other people's productions (beliefs, tools, buildings) to further their own goals, which sometimes leads to transmission and rarely to cumulative processes.
