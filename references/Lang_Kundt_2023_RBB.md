# Lang and Kundt (2023) The Evolution of Human Ritual Behavior as a Cooperative Signaling Platform

#type/paper #theme/ritual #theme/cooperation #theme/hbe #journal/RBB #theory/costly_signaling #theory/complex_signaling_systems

**Bibliography**: Lang, Martin, and Radek Kundt. 2023. "The Evolution of Human Ritual Behavior as a Cooperative Signaling Platform." *Religion, Brain & Behavior*. DOI: 10.1080/2153599X.2023.2197977

## Topic

This target article advances an evolutionary account of collective ritual as a *cooperative signaling platform* — a complex signaling system through which hominins communicated cooperative intentions before and alongside the development of symbolic language. The central puzzle motivating the paper is the omnipresence of resource-costly collective ritual across human societies despite its apparent immediate fitness costs. The authors argue that an adequate ultimate explanation requires tracing the evolutionary pathway of ritualized signals from fixed-index primate communication to the complex multi-modal system observed in contemporary humans.

## Key Theory / Framework

The authors adopt Rappaport's (1999, p. 24) structural definition of ritual as "the performance of more or less invariant sequences of formal acts and utterances not entirely encoded by the performers." Following Rappaport, they treat ritual as creating indexical relationships between the material signifier (e.g., number of pigs sacrificed) and an immaterial significatum (e.g., degree of commitment to subsequent cooperative action), such that ritual performance cannot help but reliably indicate the intensity of the performer's support for the social order and its cooperative stipulations.

The authors situate collective ritual within the framework of **complex signaling systems** (Barker et al., 2019; Hebets et al., 2016), arguing that ritual signals are redundant (repeated confirmation of commitment), degenerate (multiple channels conveying the same message), and pluripotent (a single signal conveying multiple messages). To explain how this complexity evolved, they reverse-engineer the signaling system into three ideal-type component signals, each with a distinct evolutionary origin:

> **Similarity signals** indicate shared interests between signaler and receiver (e.g., movement mirroring, synchronous dance). The authors predict these evolved earliest, exapted from the high-fidelity imitation mechanisms underlying the Acheulean Industrial Complex, to facilitate cooperation among non-kin. Selective pressure: increasing group size requiring cooperation with less-related individuals.

> **Coalitional signals** indicate membership in a specific group or cooperative ensemble (e.g., group-specific gestures, body pigmentation, ritual markings). These require shared intentionality and recursion — the ability for A to know that B knows they share a communicative code. Selective pressure: large-game hunting requiring coordinated collective action.

> **Commitment signals** indicate the graded strength of obligation to a particular risky joint action (e.g., self-inflicted pain, resource sacrifice). These apply **costly signaling theory** (Grafen, 1990; Zahavi & Zahavi, 1999): signals are honest because the cost of production ensures only genuinely committed signalers find it profitable. Selective pressure: collective action with high free-riding risk and between-group conflict.

The three signals are treated as ideal types with distinct evolutionary pathways that later coalesce into the complex signaling system of collective ritual, co-evolving with symbolic communication and eventually becoming normatively expected performances.

## Data & Methods

This is a theoretical synthesis paper (target article). It contains no original empirical data. The authors cross-corroborate evidence from four disciplinary sources:

- **Primate ethology**: review of cooperative signals in non-human primates as an analog for the ancestral signal pool
- **Archaeology**: material record of ritualized signals in the African hominin lineage (pigment use, hunting toolkits, cave art)
- **Neurocognitive science**: dating the presence of key neural structures (mirror-neuron system, theory of mind, mental time travel) via behavioral proxies in the fossil and lithic record
- **Ethnography**: illustrative examples of the three signal types in contemporary hunter-gatherer and small-scale societies (!Kung San, Andamanese, Blackfoot)

The authors explicitly restrict their timeline to the **African continental record** to remain conservative and avoid assuming traits evolved outside Africa and migrated in.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Non-human primate cooperative signals are rudimentary

The authors identify structural analogs to all three signal types in non-human primates but characterize them as rudimentary and playing only a minor role in facilitating mutualistic cooperation. Chimpanzee pant-hoot chorusing represents the closest analog to similarity signals; dyadic coalitionary recruitment uses innate rather than invented signals; commitment-like signals (e.g., genital fondling in male Guinea baboons and macaques as risky re-assurance; testicle handling in chimpanzees) exist but the cost is not graded. These signals appear across simian taxa, suggesting they may have been in the common ancestor and constituted the exaptive pool from which hominid ritualized signals were molded.

### Finding 2: Archaeological evidence is biased toward the Upper Paleolithic

The oldest documented similarity signals (cave art depicting dance, Borneo, 20 ka) and commitment signals (artistic depictions of body modification, 30 ka) skew to the Upper Paleolithic. The authors argue this reflects the material durability of evidence rather than actual emergence dates: gestural signals and body modifications leave no direct archaeological trace. The most durable early evidence — pigment (red ochre) use for coalitional signaling — extends to 285 ka in Kenya and to 500 ka in South Africa (Fauersmith industry, Watts et al., 2016), suggesting the early signal record is substantially older than the symbolic Upper Paleolithic.

### Finding 3: Similarity signals likely emerged ~1.76 Ma with H. ergaster

High-fidelity imitation required for similarity signals can be inferred from the transmission of the Acheulean Industrial Complex (earliest evidence 1.76 Ma in West Turkana, Kenya), which involves causally opaque actions that require action imitation. The Acheulean spread to South Africa by 1.6 Ma. The authors argue that the co-option of overimitation for signaling aligned interests — exapting the same mechanism used to copy toolmaking movements — would have been available to H. ergaster. The selective pressure was a novel meta-group network of ~100 members with individual foraging bands of 15–28 individuals, constituting increased need to cooperate with less-related individuals.

### Finding 4: Coalitional signals likely emerged ~550–300 ka with H. heidelbergensis

The cognitive prerequisites for coalitional signals (shared intentionality, recursion, theory of mind) are inferred from: (a) blade-producing core reduction technology in East Africa dated to 550 ka (Johnson & McBrearty, 2010), which was likely transmitted via teaching; (b) the Levallois industry, widespread in Africa after 300 ka (Lycett et al., 2016), also transmitted via teaching. A material proxy for coordinated collective action (hafted stone spear tips, Kathu Plain, South Africa, 500 ka; Wilkins et al., 2012) coincides with the first appearance of pigment use. The selective pressure was increased reliance on collectively acquired meat by H. heidelbergensis. Fire use, documented at home bases from ~400 ka (Qesem cave, Israel) and widespread in Africa by 350 ka, would have enabled communal night performance of ritualized dyadic signals, catalyzing their conventionalization into group-specific traditions via conformist social learning.

### Finding 5: Commitment signals likely emerged ~300 ka with archaic H. sapiens

The neurocognitive prerequisite for commitment signals — mental time travel (the ability to simulate others' future utility calculations) — is inferred from long-distance transport (50 km) of obsidian as raw material for knapping in Kenya (~300 ka; Brooks et al., 2018). Shorter-range tool transport in the Oldowan industry (<10 km) does not require future simulation. The period around 300 ka coincides with the transition from H. heidelbergensis to archaic H. sapiens (Hublin et al., 2017), resource patchiness caused by variable wet-dry cycles in East Africa, megafaunal turnover, and a sudden decrease in effective African population size (Bergström et al., 2020) that would have increased between-group contact and inter-group conflict. The authors cite Sosis et al. (2007) as empirical support: inter-tribal warfare intensity is positively associated with intensity of commitment signals across ethnographic societies.

### Finding 6: Collective ritual conjectured to have emerged as a coalescence ~300 ka

Synthesizing the three signal timelines (Figure 1 in the paper), the authors conjecture that collective ritual as a stable trait — the regular, staged, multi-modal performance of all three signals in a one-to-many format — emerged around 300 ka. This model differs from prior single-cause accounts (Rossano, 2009; Sosis & Alcorta, 2003; Sterelny, 2017; Turner et al., 2017) in reconstructing ritual from multiple dyadic signals each under distinct selective pressures, only later coalescing via feedback with symbolic communication and normative enforcement.

### Finding 7: Alternative explanations are addressed but not treated as primary

The authors assess four alternatives: (a) ritual as social bonding via collective effervescence (Durkheim, Rossano) — accepted as a *consequence* of ritual but derived from signaling function; (b) communicative pluripotency (status, wealth, mate quality signals) — accepted as co-present messages but cooperative intention held as primary; (c) ritual as extension of individual anxiety-reducing ritual (Boyer & Liénard, 2006; Lang et al., 2015) — the public, costly, staged nature of collective ritual is held to require the cooperative signaling explanation; (d) ritual as spandrel — the authors acknowledge this possibility while maintaining that the evidence supports functional adaptation.
