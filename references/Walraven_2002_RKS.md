# Walraven (2002) Weavers of Ritual: How Shamans Achieve Their Aims

#type/paper #theme/ritual #theme/korean_shamanism #theme/religious_practice #journal/RKS #theory/rational_choice_religion

**Bibliography**: Walraven, Boudewijn. 2002. "Weavers of Ritual: How Shamans Achieve Their Aims." *The Review of Korean Studies* 5(1):85–104.

## Topic

Applying the rational actor axiom and focusing on ritual practice rather than idealized or reified traditions, the article asks how the clients of Korean shamans who perform in the Hwanghae and Seoul styles derive benefits from the rituals (*kut*) they commission. It does so by examining the "small print" of these rituals — the concrete details of ritual action and their impact on participants. The central question is how shamans make their rituals meaningful, and thereby effective, to individual clients, proceeding from the hypothesis that clients have good reasons to have such rituals performed. The author frames this as also a plea for a certain approach to ritual in general, and as a contribution to understanding why such rituals continue to be performed in 21st-century Seoul and its satellite cities.

## Key Theory / Framework

The paper adopts the **rational actor axiom** as extended to religion by Rodney Stark and Roger Finke in *Acts of Faith* (Stark & Finke 2000:42, 55): the assumption that human beings do not act arbitrarily but attempt to achieve desirable goals, and that believers are persons making calculated choices aimed at maximal returns (material or spiritual) for their efforts, rather than misguided victims of false consciousness. Walraven sympathizes with taking believers seriously (citing Bell 1997) but notes that Stark and Finke largely ignore less-organized, non-Western forms of religion; he applies the axiom precisely to such a case.

The framework's central move is a **consistent focus on religious practice** rather than theology or culturally determined ideals. This is set explicitly against the folkloristic and heritage-oriented Korean scholarship that reifies *kut* as authentic "tradition" and makes the person of the shaman, or the identification of a national "Korean pantheon," the focus of study (a stance the author illustrates with Son Chin-t'ae's 1936 view of shamans as survivals of a "primitive past," and with a critique of the "nationalization" of shamanism as intangible cultural heritage). Reframed as practice, a "good" shaman is not one who faithfully transmits past forms but one whose ritual is meaningful to — that is, affects — the clients and is therefore effective.

The author situates his account against and alongside several theories of ritual. Against Frits Staal's claim that ritual is intrinsically meaningless and its actions have no expressive function (Staal 1975), he argues that in *kut*, where performers and clients constantly interact, all participants are engaged in the creation and transmission of meaning. Following Tambiah (1985), he holds that the actual benefits of ritual need not be its ostensible aims (pregnancy, business success, passing an examination), but include other, unspoken positive effects. He characterizes the effective *kut* as a **psychological transaction** in which clients are liberated from worries and given confidence to face future challenges — the recurrent emic criterion being *shiwon-hada*, "to feel relieved" (antonym *taptap-hada*). On possession, he engages Ioan Lewis's *Ecstatic Religion*: whereas Lewis reads possession in marginal cults as giving voice and power to the oppressed, Walraven argues that in Korea this is not its central function; possession instead furnishes the culturally defined ritual structure that can effect a transformation in the client (Walraven 1993a).

## Data & Methods

This is an **interpretive/ethnographic study based on participant observation**, not a quantitative study; there are no datasets, samples, or statistics in the survey sense.

- **Data source**: the author's own fieldwork observations of shamanic rituals (*kut*).
- **Ritual styles**: Hwanghae-do and Hanyang (Seoul) styles.
- **Region**: rituals observed in Seoul and Inch'ŏn (with named sites including the Map'o Pugundang, the P'ŏmbawi *kuttang* on Namsan, and *kuttang* on Tobongsan, Kanghwa-do, Ansan, Kyeyangsan, and near Inch'ŏn).
- **Time period**: mostly 1999–2000, with references to *kut* witnessed earlier (dated examples range from 1991 and 1992 through 2000).
- **Funding**: a Korea Foundation Fellowship.
- **Method**: close observation of ritual detail (the "small print"), attentive to how general folkloric forms are individualized for particular clients; the author acknowledges indebtedness to discussions with, and the work of, Antonetta Bruno (*The Gate of Words*, Bruno 2001).

Ritual types drawn on as observed cases include *hŏju kut* (a first step toward becoming a shaman, removing bothersome spirits), *naerim kut* (initiation), *aekttaem* (a ritual to avoid mishaps), *nullim kut* (a "suppression" ritual to prevent someone from becoming a shaman), *chinjŏk kut* (a shaman's ritual of thanks to her gods), *pyŏng kut* (a healing ritual), and death rituals.

## Key Findings

The paper's "findings" are interpretive claims about the mechanisms through which *kut* becomes meaningful and effective, each grounded in specific observed ritual episodes rather than in numerical estimates.

### Finding 1: The effective *kut* is a psychological transaction organized around relief (*shiwon-hada*)

For shamans and clients alike, the criterion of a good *kut* is that the client be liberated from worries and given confidence to face the future, even when the ostensible aim (pregnancy, business success, passing an examination) is harder to secure (Bruno 2001:160–161). The recurring term is *shiwon-hada* (to feel relieved). In a *hŏju kut* (Tobongsan, 14 April 2000) held to free a young boy of troublesome spirits, the grandmother's descended spirit promised him relief (*shiwon-hae chugetta*) and, using the antonym *taptap-hada*, said she had caused the family worries because they had ignored her, but that now all would be well.

### Finding 2: Experienced shamans consciously work on clients' feelings, adapting ritual format to release emotion

At a death ritual for a 35-year-old man who had died suddenly in his sleep (P'ŏmbawi *kuttang*, Namsan, Seoul, 12 July 1992), his wife and sister were at first locked in silent grief; the shamans deliberately prolonged the Confucian-style *chesa*, urging the women to speak to the deceased, and only when a shaman told the sister to give her brother a cigarette (he had been a fond smoker) did the "dams break" and emotion pour out. The author reads this as the shamans letting the *chesa* continue until the women could release pent-up emotion, without which they would not have immersed themselves as fully in the later, more dramatic parts (in which the shaman reenacted the death agony and spoke as the deceased). Because Korean sentence mood is only clear at the end, and because possession lets otherwise unsayable emotion be voiced, the shaman can address whichever emotional register matters most to the client.

### Finding 3: The pantheon functions as a catalogue of human emotions

Full-scale rituals devote attention to supernatural beings of very different characters — serene deities, frightening or vengeful ones, imperious generals, venal death messengers, bloodthirsty *kunung* (spirits of the violently dead), and endearing child spirits. Through the variety of personae assumed in possession, the shaman can "play" on the client's different emotional registers. In a *naerim kut* (Kanghwa-do, 14–15 May 2000), a shaman possessed by the spirit of a car-repair-shop manager who had burned alive when an oil drum exploded rolled screaming across the floor in pain, unleashing violent emotion in the man's sister and mother, followed by a poignant dialogue once the shaman had calmed.

### Finding 4: Food, cloth, and money are the media that individualize ritual and involve the client's body

The most important media of communication are food, cloth, and money; besides being offerings, they transmit the *kut*'s effect to the clients' bodies — women catch chestnuts and fruit in their skirts, shamans rub or wave cloth to blow blessings toward them, and rice is ingested to "incorporate" blessings. Money is not merely payment: only part is paid beforehand and the rest handed over during the *kut*, keeping its specific purpose visible and making the quid-pro-quo persuasive; it is the client's money that makes the ritual hers rather than anyone's. Hidden 10,000-*wŏn* bills "charge" ritual objects (a bride's head ornament, the paper "dressing" the bronze *myŏngdu* mirrors, the drum, the grain measure beneath the straw-cutters). The *kunungtae* — a Hwanghae "weapon" for hunting the *kunung* spirits — is wrapped outside with white and yellowish cloth of the dead but packed inside with the clients' own clothing (a Mickey Mouse T-shirt for the son, a Pierre Cardin blouse for the mother, a singlet for the father), or, absent clothing, paper bearing a person's name and birth date. Clothes personalize in other ways too: an absent 10-year-old boy's sweatshirt was placed under the shaman's feet on the razor-sharp straw-cutters in an *aekttaem* (13 February 2000); silk clothes are made for the dead; extra sets are ordered from the *manmulsang* for *chosang* (ancestors) discovered mid-ritual (ten sets ordered by phone in the *aekttaem* case).

### Finding 5: Divinatory acts and spectacular feats reassure clients, demarcate ritual space, and are themselves personalized

Divinatory actions — *sashil* (balancing a pig's carcass on a trident), *sanssal* (counting rice grains scooped with the bells), choosing colored flags, circling a rice bowl without spilling grains, and *mul sashil* (water divination, e.g., basins balanced improbably on a wine bottle, Kyeyangsan, 7 April 2000) — reassure clients that things go well and suggest that ordinary limits can be suspended. Non-divinatory feats (mounting the razor-sharp straw-cutters, lifting heavy brass basins or tied-together tables with the teeth) are read as possible only under inspiration (*shinparam-e*), heightening the shaman's authority and the clients' confidence. Mounting the straw-cutters (*chaktu t'agi*) is paradigmatic for personalization: the elevation may be built from the client's own donated rice bags and grain, money the client donated is buried in the grain, a family member's garment is laid on the knives for protection, and grains thrown up and caught (when even in number) are ingested by clients, as are the cigarettes the mounted "General" has smoked and the water from beneath the knives.

### Finding 6: Verbal means, above all *kongsu*, personalize the ritual through two-way, performative speech

*Kongsu* (oracles), in which deities address clients through the shaman's mouth, form the core of every ritual (Guillemoz 1994; Bruno 1998, 2001). Drawing on Bruno, the author stresses that *kongsu* is not one-way: it demands the client's involvement and carries information in both directions, so the shaman also learns and can personalize further. Many recurrent phrases are "performative" — as reassurances, promises, and pledges their utterance is itself an act designed to influence the client's psyche — and because *kongsu* nimbly alternates the voices of deity and shaman, and Korean sentence mood surfaces only at the end, the difference between a shaman's wish and a deity's promise is obscured, obliterating the gap between wish and fulfillment (Bruno 2001:129). Beyond *kongsu*, shamans build confidence through talk of instructive dreams and of worshipped spirits who were their own accomplished deceased relatives; deity paintings (*hwan*/*maji*) and music further create and mark the ritual atmosphere and signal the *kut*'s phases (Bruno 2001).

### Finding 7: The shaman is an "equilibrist" balancing fixed structure against personalization, weaving a few simple metaphors

A *kut* is a precarious balancing act between maintaining a distinct ritual structure and adapting parts to the client's needs; both the fixity and the flexibility contribute to its success. A fixed, prescribed structure sets the ritual off from daily life, "disorienting" clients so they may be re-oriented toward a brighter future and encouraging them to entrust their problems to the *mudang*, at least temporarily lessening their burden. Flexibility and rule-keeping are not exclusive: when one rule cannot be observed, a more obscure one is invoked (in heavy rain at a death ritual near Inch'ŏn, 31 October 1999, everyone put a left sock on the head for protection). The complexity that marks the *kut* as extra-mundane coexists with underlying simplicity, because the same few principles recur — notably two versatile metaphors, *putt'a* (attaching) and *p'ulda* (unbinding/dissolving). "Ninety-nine bells" made to stick to and lift an apple is a good omen; the *kwishin* stuck to the *hŏju kut* boy must be sent away; in a *pyŏng kut* (P'ŏmbawi *kuttang*, 12 July 1991) a shaman transferred illness into knotted cloth, undid the knots (*ko p'uri*), then tore the cloth around the old man's body (as in *kil karŭgi*, "parting of the ways" of living and dead). Playfulness (*giocoso*/*scherzando*) is crucial — candy for child spirits doubles as a light-hearted divination. The efficacy of *kut*, the author concludes, lies in the dexterity with which a small number of fundamentally simple principles is woven together, balancing adherence to ritual rules and personal adaptation so as to affect the clients maximally (their "perlocutionary force").
