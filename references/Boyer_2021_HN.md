# Boyer (2021) Deriving Features of Religions in the Wild: How Communication and Threat-Detection May Predict Spirits, Gods, Witches, and Shamans

**Bibliography**: Boyer, Pascal. 2021. "Deriving Features of Religions in the Wild: How Communication and Threat-Detection May Predict Spirits, Gods, Witches, and Shamans." *Human Nature* 32:557–581. https://doi.org/10.1007/s12110-021-09410-y

## Topic

This is a theoretical paper proposing a speculative model to explain why "religions in the wild" — pre-doctrinal and non-organized religious traditions (shamanism, ancestor cults, witchcraft, divination) — share three cross-cultural recurrent features across otherwise diverse societies. The puzzle is that these wild traditions focus overwhelmingly on specific cases of misfortune, attribute misfortune to superhuman agency, and concentrate services in specialists construed as inherently different from ordinary people. Boyer argues that two independently established components of human evolved psychology — epistemic vigilance and threat-detection — generate communication dynamics that predict these features without invoking group-level selection or doctrinal transmission.

## Key Theory / Framework

Boyer distinguishes **wild traditions** (pre-doctrinal religious activities in small-scale societies or at the margins of organized religions) from **organized doctrinal religions**, listing six contrasts: no central doctrine, practitioners are individuals rather than credentialed personnel, the goal is particular cases not salvation, no unified services, superhuman agents are local, no membership or commitment requirement.

The paper frames recurrent features of wild traditions as **cultural attractors** — particular combinations of representations more likely to occur in similar form across times and places, either through selection among alternatives or through inference and active reconstruction toward cognitively stable variants (Claidière et al., 2014; Sperber & Hirschfeld, 2004). It then identifies two psychological systems as the mechanistic basis:

**Epistemic vigilance** is the suite of cognitive capacities geared to maximizing the benefits from useful information and detecting deception (Sperber et al., 2010; Mercier & Sperber, 2017). Key features invoked:
1. Epistemic vigilance systems are automatically activated when information is received.
2. Source evaluation is crucial; people have vastly more information about a shaman as a source than about inscrutable processes like witchcraft.
3. Competence is construed as a stable trait; providing valuable information in the past predicts valuable information in the future, a component of social status (Henrich & Gil-White, 2001).

**Threat-detection psychology** is the set of evolved cognitive responses to direct and potential fitness threats (Boyer & Bergstrom, 2011). Features invoked:
- Humans are predisposed to receive most threat-related information from others rather than from individual learning [Feature 4].
- Negativity bias: people pay special attention to negative information; threat-related information is attended to, remembered, and transmitted preferentially (Baumeister et al., 2001; Blaine & Boyer, 2018).
- Risk-sensitivity: when potential costs are high and individual need absolute, people favor high-risk/high-potential-benefit strategies even with low probability of success [Feature 5].
- Error management: when cost of missing a real threat exceeds cost of false alarm, people over-detect and take precautionary action despite epistemic uncertainty [Feature 6] (Haselton & Buss, 2000).

The **Good Source model** is the speculative communication-dynamics model built on these foundations. It is formulated for a simplified ancestral small-group situation (cohesive community, face-to-face acquaintance, no mass communication, rudimentary technology):

> *[Hypothesis 1]* There are benefits to having Good Sources (GS) in one's epistemic environment.

> *[Hypothesis 2]* There are benefits to being seen as a Good Source in other people's epistemic environment.

> *[Hypothesis 3]* The probability that a Listener engages in a recommended course of action is a direct function of the Listener's estimate of the Source's general epistemic quality.

> *[Hypothesis 4]* The perceived quality of a Source, for a Listener, is a function of the number of third parties who follow the Source's recommendations.

Hypotheses 3 and 4 together generate **behavioral cascades**: the fact that one person follows a Source's advice makes that Source more authoritative in others' eyes, who then become more likely to follow, providing further evidence to yet more people. The model differs from standard cultural evolution frameworks (Boyd & Richerson, 1985; Claidière et al., 2018) in that those frameworks explain the *reception and consumption* of cultural information, while the Good Source model aims to explain the *production* — why people are motivated to formulate and broadcast threat-related explanations of misfortune in the first place.

## Data & Methods

This is a purely theoretical paper with no original data collection. The evidentiary base consists of (a) independently established findings from experimental psychology (cited studies on epistemic vigilance, negativity bias, error management, cultural transmission experiments); (b) the cross-cultural anthropological ethnographic record (Evans-Pritchard on the Azande, Winkelman's HRAF-based comparative survey of magico-religious practitioners, Fessler et al.'s systematic study of threat material in religious traditions); and (c) formal models from cultural evolution and economics. No sample size or dataset applies. Boyer explicitly notes the model is speculative and that confirming it will require large-scale quantitative comparisons.

## Key Findings

### Recurrent features 1–3: The explanandum

Boyer identifies three recurrent features of wild traditions requiring explanation. **Feature 1**: focus on particular cases of misfortune (not general principles of causation); shamans cure specific people, ancestors are propitiated about specific failures, witchcraft explains why *this* person got ill at *this* time. **Feature 2**: all specific misfortune is attributed to the actions of superhuman agents — ancestors, spirits, gods, ghosts, witches — construed with specific intentionality. **Feature 3**: religious specialization is universal; only some individuals provide diagnostic information and offer solutions; specialists are construed as inherently different from ordinary people through essential internal qualities, not technical training (Winkelman, 1986, 1990).

### Evidence for epistemic vigilance and threat transmission

Boyer cites Boyer & Parren (2015): a tour guide who warns of trekking dangers is judged more "competent" about the region than one who mentions other features; this is not a general negativity effect since negative non-threat information does not similarly raise competence ratings. Fessler et al. (2014) and Hilbig (2009, 2012) independently found that people assign higher subjective probability to negatively framed than positively framed statements about the same event (e.g., "5% of heart attack victims die within ten years" judged more plausible than "95% survive"). Blaine & Boyer (2018), using an artificial cultural transmission paradigm across five generations of participants, found that threat information is chosen for transmission more often than positive, neutral, or even negative non-threat information about the same topics, and is transmitted better even when described as extremely low probability. Fessler et al. (2014) provide a systematic study confirming threat material occurs more often than positive-reward messages across most religious traditions.

### Prediction 1: Focus of messages will be specific cases of misfortune

Follows from the GS model because positive outcomes do not afford the same Good Source valuation opportunities as negative-threat-related messages. Supported by the cross-cultural ethnographic record — wild religious activity and communication focus on misfortune (ancestors involved in illness or accidents, shamans cure specific ailments, spirits protect against specific failures).

### Prediction 2: Explanations focus on inscrutable facts or processes

The GS model predicts that messages in which the information is not put to the test have lower epistemic cost to the Speaker without equivalent loss of potential benefits. Inscrutable causal processes (spirits throwing invisible darts, souls being stolen by undetectable means) are epistemically triggering for vigilance systems yet are bypassed by threat-detection under high-perceived-cost conditions. Boyer connects this to the anthropological observation (Needham, 1976) that claims of causal connections leave causal processes opaque, and to Sperber's (1997, 2000) argument that meta-represented beliefs ("it is true that p") can be held without commitment to specific implications.

### Prediction 3: Explanations will be specific, precautions may be generic

Boyer argues there is a reputational asymmetry between specific and general statements. A general principle ("black cats cause miscarriages") can be disseminated once and then belongs to common knowledge — no further reputational benefit accrues to the original speaker. But specific diagnoses of unique cases require the specialist to re-demonstrate authority for each instance, continuously re-generating GS status. Precautions, by contrast, are not subject to this limitation because they are never falsified under error-management logic, so they can be highly generic. This predicts the observed pattern in many cultures: highly specific prescriptions (wear a vial of water from the River Jordan) combined with extremely vague descriptions of what they guard against (illness, accidents, misfortune in general).

### Prediction 4: Perceived differences lead to specialization

Initial differences in perceived GS quality — even very small — are amplified by cascade dynamics (Hypotheses 3 + 4). The Speaker with slightly higher initial reputation receives more followers, increasing perceived quality for yet more observers, leading to larger status disparities. This eventually makes it irrational for low-reputation Speakers to even enter the market for explaining misfortune, generating a division between specialists and laypeople. Boyer notes that Singh (2018) identifies the rare human capacity for controlled trance states as one mechanism by which shamans worldwide maintain and emphasize their essential distinctiveness.

### Prediction 5: Specialists are construed in essentialist terms

Because cascade dynamics are opaque to participants (each person simply decides to trust a Source based on others' decisions without perceiving the aggregate dynamic), people invoke "folk-sociology" to explain the observed specialization — namely, essentialist attributions of inherent, often inherited, internal qualities that distinguish specialists from ordinary individuals. Boyer cites Winkelman's (1986, 1990) cross-cultural survey documenting that shaman- and healer-like practitioners are universally construed as possessing inherent qualities; in many African contexts interpreted as possession of an extra organ; in more abstract forms as special psychological capacities (Stépanoff, 2015). This is the most widespread explanation for specialist authority in wild traditions.

### The production/consumption asymmetry

Boyer argues this is the paper's main novel contribution relative to prior cognitive science of religion: prior models explain why threatening, counterintuitive, or agent-involving representations are *memorable* and *believable* (consumption side), but not why people are motivated to *produce and broadcast* them. The GS model supplies the production-side motivation: threat-related messages, when adopted by even cautious Listeners, enhance the Speaker's GS reputation, which is valuable because Good Sources attract cooperation benefits (Hypothesis 1) and being seen as one is therefore beneficial (Hypothesis 2).
