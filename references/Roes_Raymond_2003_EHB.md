# Roes and Raymond (2003) Belief in Moralizing Gods

#type/paper #theme/csr #theme/cooperation #theme/hbe #journal/EHB #theory/alexander_morality #theory/moralizing_gods

**Bibliography**: Roes, Frans L., and Michel Raymond. 2003. "Belief in Moralizing Gods." *Evolution and Human Behavior* 24(2):126–135.

## Topic

This paper tests four hypotheses derived from Alexander's (1987) theory of morality using cross-cultural data from the Ethnographic Atlas and the Standard Cross-Cultural Sample. The central argument is that between-group competition drives increases in society size, that larger societies face greater fission pressure, and that moralizing gods — as authoritative, impartial, and immortal enforcers of moral rules — are an effective mechanism for suppressing internal conflict in large societies. All four hypotheses receive empirical support, providing the first statistical cross-cultural confirmation of the society size–moralizing gods association.

## Key Theory / Framework

The paper is grounded in **Alexander's (1987) theory of morality**, which holds that human group size expanded primarily because of between-group competition: other human groups, not predators or resources per se, constituted the principal selective pressure for larger coalitions. In rich habitats, larger groups outcompete smaller ones, displacing them to ecological margins. Larger groups, however, face intensified fission pressure as internal conflicts of interest multiply. Moral rules — prescriptions against infringements on others' rights — are Alexander's proposed mechanism for holding large groups together. The paper adds that because human-imposed moral rules are suspect (beneficiaries may be motivated to impose rules selectively), **moralizing gods** — portrayed as impartial, non-material, and immortal — serve as a particularly effective vehicle for inviolable moral authority. Publicly believing in such gods signals acceptance of the moral order.

The authors also evaluate two alternative explanations:

> **Marxist/stratification explanation** (Irons, 1991; Cronk, 1994): in stratified societies, moral rules presented as divine commands protect the privileges of the powerful. The authors test this and find it partially supported for caste stratification but not for class stratification, so Alexander's and the Marxist explanations are held to co-apply.

> **Ecological/water scarcity explanation** (Snarey, 1996): dry environments require moral norms protecting shared water resources, generating moralizing gods. The authors replicate Snarey's finding and show it holds independently of society size — but water scarcity does not predict society size, distinguishing this pathway from Alexander's.

Finally, the paper notes that its test of the resource base → external conflict → society size chain constitutes, to the authors' knowledge, the first statistical test of **Carneiro's (1970) circumscription theory** of the origin of the state.

## Data & Methods

- **Data sources**: Ethnographic Atlas (EA), 1967 edition (Murdock; 862 societies), 1990 edition (White; 1267 societies), and 1999 edition (Gray; 1267 societies); Standard Cross-Cultural Sample (SCCS) 1986 and 1995 editions (186 societies)
- **Country/region**: worldwide sample; six geographical regions (Africa, Circum-Mediterranean, East Eurasia, Insular Pacific, North America, South America) used as control
- **N**: EA 1999: up to 1267 societies; SCCS: 186 societies; specific test Ns range from 85 to 724 depending on variable availability
- **Unit of analysis**: society (ethnographic unit)
- **Dependent variable**: Moralizing Gods — EA variable 34 / SCCS variable 238 ("High Gods"), recoded binary: (1) absent/present but not active in human affairs/present but not supportive of human morality vs. (2) present, active, and specifically supportive of human morality
- **Key independent variable**: Society size — EA variable 33 / SCCS variable 237 (jurisdictional hierarchy beyond local community, 5-level ordinal: 1 = no authority beyond community, 5 = four levels, e.g., large states)
- **Additional independent variables**:
  - Resource base — SCCS variable 859, recoded to 3 groups: low (hunting/gathering/fishing), unstable (mounted hunting/shifting cultivation/horticulture), high (intensive agriculture/pastoralism)
  - External conflict — composite mean of 6 SCCS 1986 variables (774, 780, 783, 892, 893, 907); for SCCS 1995, variables 1650 and 1653
  - Internal conflict — composite mean of 6 SCCS 1986 variables (666, 767, 768, 770, 773, 891); for SCCS 1995, variables 1649 and 1652
  - Region — SCCS variable 200 / EA variables 90–91
- **Controls tested**: region, classical religion (SCCS variable 713), population density (SCCS variable 64), class stratification (EA variable 65), caste stratification (EA variable 67)
- **Method**: Kendall rank-order correlation (τ) for bivariate associations; Kendall partial rank-order correlation (Moran, 1951) to control for third variables; multinomial linear model (GLIM version 4) for simultaneous control of multiple confounders; overdispersion corrected where present; all P-values two-tailed
- **Identification strategy**: Cross-cultural comparative method. Galton's problem (non-independence from cultural diffusion) addressed by relying on the SCCS (constructed to represent independent cultural types); robustness checked across multiple EA editions

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Resource richness predicts external conflict (Hypothesis I)

Resource base correlates positively with external conflict: Kendall's τ = 0.16 (P = .01, n = 176) in SCCS 1986; τ = 0.21 (P = .002, n = 154) in SCCS 1995. The association remains significant when region is controlled (P < .01). This supports Alexander's (1987) argument that richer habitats concentrate intergroup competition.

### Finding 2: External conflict predicts society size (Hypothesis II)

External conflict correlates positively with society size: τ = 0.23 (P < 10⁻⁴, n = 174) in SCCS 1986; τ = 0.22 (P = .001, n = 152) in SCCS 1995. Region-controlled results remain significant (P < 10⁻⁴). The chain resource richness → external conflict → larger society is consistent across editions.

### Finding 3: Resource richness predicts society size (Hypothesis III)

Resource base correlates positively with society size: τ = 0.48 (P < 10⁻⁴, n = 184) in SCCS. This is the strongest bivariate correlation in the paper. The authors note this result supports Carneiro's (1970) circumscription theory of state origin, which they claim is the first statistical test of that theory.

### Finding 4: Society size predicts moralizing gods (Hypothesis IV, base result)

Society size correlates positively with belief in moralizing gods: τ = 0.29 (P < 10⁻⁴, n = 167) in SCCS; τ = 0.37 (P < 10⁻⁴, n = 724) in EA 1999. Essentially identical results appear in EA 1967 and EA 1990 editions. The relationship is visualized in Figure 1: at society size 1 (no political authority beyond community), moralizing gods are rare; at size 5 (four levels, large states), they are the majority.

### Finding 5: Hypothesis IV survives controls for region, classical religion, and population density

Controlling for region: P < 10⁻⁴. Controlling for classical religion (SCCS variable 713): partial Kendall's τ = 0.16 (P = .031, n = 85). Controlling for population density (SCCS variable 64): partial Kendall's τ = 0.31 (P < 10⁻⁴, n = 165). Society size retains independent predictive power for moralizing gods after removing these alternative explanations.

### Finding 6: Caste stratification (but not class stratification) also predicts moralizing gods — Marxist explanation partially supported

Society size is highly correlated with EA variable 65 (class stratification): τ = 0.51 (P < 10⁻⁴, n = 1025); and with EA variable 67 (caste stratification): τ = 0.32 (P < 10⁻⁴, n = 1017). However, in a multinomial linear model controlling simultaneously for class stratification, caste stratification, and region (EA 1990, N = 591), the society size × moralizing gods interaction remains significant (χ² = 20.6, df = 4, P = .0004). Within this model, the class stratification × moralizing gods interaction is not significant (P > .8), but the caste stratification × moralizing gods interaction is (P = .0054). The authors conclude that both Alexander's and the Marxist explanation are empirically supported, with the Marxist account applying specifically to caste (endogamous, extreme) stratification.

### Finding 7: No evidence that moralizing gods reduce internal conflict

The expected reduction in internal conflict due to moralizing gods is not observed. In SCCS 1986: τ = 0.002 (P = .98, n = 157); in SCCS 1995: τ = 0.019 (P = .80, n = 139); partial τ controlling for reliability of internal warfare ratings: −0.012 (P = .83, n = 138). The authors speculate that rational individuals follow moral rules only when social sanctions are strong or when the external threat is immediate, so an unconditional reduction in internal conflict would not be expected without information on when collective action is perceived as vital.

### Finding 8: Water scarcity independently predicts moralizing gods (Snarey replication)

Recoding SCCS variable 855 (niche rainfall) into dry vs. wet: τ = 0.385 (P < 10⁻³, n = 168) with moralizing gods. This correlation survives sequential controls for society size, population density, region, classical religion, and stratification. However, water scarcity does not predict society size, distinguishing this ecological pathway from Alexander's competition pathway. The authors treat the two as complementary: both recurring intergroup threats and recurring drought independently generate moralizing gods.

## Scholarly Conversation

- **builds on**: Alexander (1987, *The Biology of Moral Systems*) — foundational theory tested here
- **alternative**: Snarey (1996, *JSSR*) — ecological/water-scarcity explanation; the authors replicate and extend rather than reject this
- **alternative**: Swanson (1960, *The Birth of the Gods*) — political complexity predicts high gods (not specifically moralizing); earlier non-evolutionary account
- **alternative**: Underhill (1975, *AJS*) — economic complexity predicts monotheism; the authors note this does not contradict Alexander's more general account
