# Planer and Sterelny (2026) Rise of the Dark Shaman: A Path from Healers to Sorcery

#type/paper #theme/Shamanism #theme/SorceryWitchcraft #theme/CulturalEvolution #journal/EHB #theory/erosion_of_mutualism #theory/agential_view_of_misfortune

**Bibliography**: Planer, Ronald J., and Kim Sterelny. 2026. "Rise of the Dark Shaman: A Path from Healers to Sorcery." *Evolution and Human Behavior* 47: 106912. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.evolhumbehav.2026.106912

Part of the Special Issue "Magic, Witchcraft, Myth, and Taboo."

## Topic

The paper develops a cultural evolutionary explanation for "the agential view of misfortune" — the widespread tendency in small-scale, pre-industrial societies to attribute serious misfortune (especially sudden death or severe illness) to other humans' occult activities. The phenomenon is puzzling because it is both widespread across subsistence economies and geographies, and very costly economically and socially. The paper criticizes existing explanations and proposes a novel "erosion of mutualism" model that traces the agential view through three evolutionary stages: animism → shamanism → belief in sorcerers or "dark shamans."

## Key Theory / Framework

### Erosion of Mutualism Model

The core model specifies five conditions for a cultural evolutionary process in which beliefs benefitting a powerful minority can establish at the expense of the wider community:

1. A subgroup plays an especially central role in intergenerational transmission of information, skill, and lore — they are *socially salient*.
2. Initially, this subgroup facilitates transmission of lore that is of general adaptive benefit (or at worst neutral).
3. The subgroup has shared characteristics giving them a common interest; their interests partly overlap with, but partly diverge from, the wider community's.
4. In their role as socially salient agents, they can influence community practices in ways that differentially benefit themselves, even at some cost to the community.
5. In the final phase, successive iterations of *myside bias* — the tendency to produce arguments in support of preexisting beliefs, documented extensively in cognitive psychology — shape lore transmission in ways that realize that differential benefit. Because this bias operates tacitly, each generation's version of the lore differs only slightly from its predecessor.

Applied here: shamans are the socially salient subgroup; shamanistic healing/divination is the initially beneficial lore; belief in sorcerous threats (only shamans can defend against) is the self-serving distortion that accumulates over generations.

### Three-Stage Historical Model

The model is presented as three stages expressing central tendencies, not sharply demarcated steps; transitions are gradual.

**Stage 1 — Animism**: Dreams and hallucinations, especially experiences of deceased loved ones (e.g., Grimby, 1993 found up to 80% of Euro-Americans report post-bereavement hallucinations), provide the raw material for a shared animistic worldview. Language (or a suitably rich protolanguage) enables collective interpretation and communication of these experiences. The fireside niche — the extended wakefulness created by fire control (Dunbar & Gowlett, 2014) — provided the time and social context for elaborating shared stories, myths, and animistic philosophy.

**Stage 2 — Shamans emerge**: Cognitive biases, especially *causal illusion* (the tendency to infer causal relations between variables that may not genuinely covary), lead individuals to connect their altered states of consciousness to real-world outcomes. *Placebo effects* stabilize apparent therapeutic successes. Individual variation in willingness to undergo strenuous physical, pharmacological, and psychological ordeals (starvation, seclusion, chanting until exhaustion, psychoactive drugs, self-harm) distinguishes proto-shamans: those who engage in such ordeals more often are more likely to experience extraordinary altered states, draw more causal connections, and develop beliefs in their own special powers. Credibility accrues through (a) the same cognitive biases in audience members, (b) elimination of disconfirmation opportunities once practices standardize, (c) credibility-enhancing displays (Henrich, 2009) — the sheer extremity of the ordeals rationally signals that the practitioner believes in their efficacy, and (d) accelerating social credibility curves as more community members are persuaded. Prestige hierarchies in forager societies (Henrich & Gil-White, 2001) provide additional incentive: respect for expertise yields fitness-relevant benefits.

**Stage 3 — Sorcery/dark shamans**: Once people accept the reality of shamanic powers — the ability to journey in the spirit world, exert agency there, and produce life-or-death consequences — belief in sorcerers is a trivial inferential step: all that differs is the supernatural actor's aims. The myside bias, operating unconsciously across generations, makes occult threats increasingly vivid and pressing, and the shaman's protective role increasingly indispensable. The result is an epistemic tragedy of the commons: even if all would benefit from collective skepticism, an individual turning skeptic engages in unilateral disarmament, both abandoning a potential accusatory weapon and risking accusation themselves. This produces resilience: the agential view of misfortune persists even through transitions to agriculture (and some degree of access to Western medicine), as Reid's (1983) extended case study of the Yolngu of Arnhem Land illustrates — while a majority now recognize Western medicine as superior treatment, beliefs about underlying causality have changed to a much lesser degree.

### Existing Explanations Critiqued

The paper identifies four existing explanation types as insufficient:

- **Religion adaptationism**: Proposals linking sorcery to norm compliance (e.g., Henrich, 2009; Wilson, 2002) fail because sorcerous byproduct injuries can harm innocent victims, attacks can come from within the community, and highly antisocial individuals are often the most feared sorcerers.
- **Cost asymmetries** (hypervigilance toward unseen predators or social threats): Fails because (a) Pleistocene humans were apex predators with significant predation costs from false alarms; (b) the extreme freedom children enjoy in forager societies is inconsistent with high predation risk; (c) even if social threat vigilance were adaptive, forming a *belief* in occult agents is a worse strategy than investigating hunches — a much better response to heightened social vigilance would be reinforcing affiliative links, not burying excrement.
- **Strategic accusation** (Boyer 2022; Boyer et al. 2024): Explains how the view is exploited *once entrenched*, not how it originates in the first place; does not explain why sorcery accusations are regarded as acceptable explanations in a community that doesn't already hold the view.
- **Erosion of mutualism** (their own preferred explanation): Developed in full in this paper, following the sketch in Planer & Sterelny (2024).

## Key Findings

This is a theoretical paper; findings are arguments and model components.

### Finding 1: The agential view is costly and puzzling

The agential view is widespread across subsistence economies and geographies (not tied to any one region or economy), and is very costly: economic costs of divination ceremonies and witchcraft inquests, and social costs including conflict, paranoia, and the erosion of social capital (Gershman, 2016). These costs make adaptationist explanations for the *average individual* implausible; a special evolutionary treatment is required.

### Finding 2: Existing explanations cannot account for origins

Religion adaptationism, cost asymmetry, and strategic accusation models each explain at most *maintenance* or *exploitation* of the agential view once entrenched. None adequately explains how a community would initially come to accept occult explanations of misfortune. The problem of origins is the central gap.

### Finding 3: The erosion of mutualism model has the right temporal structure

A key feature of the model is that the intergenerational apparatus of trust, deference, and respect is established while the lore is still mutually beneficial — at that stage, skepticism would be unwarranted. This temporal structure explains how a lore package that later serves minority interests can gain broad traction: by the time the myside-biased drift becomes costly, the trust infrastructure is already in place. Because each generation's drift is small, confirmation bias and related heuristics in audience members are sufficient to track the drifting norm.

### Finding 4: Shamans are a "possibility-proof" of sorcerers

Once a community accepts the seven properties of shamanic power (altered-state spirit travel, goal-directed agency in the spirit world, ability to produce life-or-death consequences, foreknowledge), belief in sorcerers requires no additional cognitive machinery. The ambiguous dual potential of shamans — as healer and potential threat — is well-documented ethnographically. Winkelman's (2021) cross-cultural study finds "In the case of Shamans, the capacity for evil is not with a different type of practitioner, but within the capacities of the Shaman" (p. 14).

### Finding 5: The view is resilient because of strategic complementarities

Once widespread, the agential view creates incentives to conform despite its costs: (a) conformity preserves access to accusation as a social lever; (b) public skepticism does not grant immunity from accusation; (c) belief signals cooperative orientation toward shamans whose goodwill matters for protection. Reid's (1983) Yolngu case — majority accept Western medicine for treatment but retain sorcery as ultimate cause — demonstrates that the view can withstand significant institutional pressure without collapsing.

### Finding 6: Empirical bets generated by the model

The model predicts (i) societies with rich shamanistic beliefs should also hold rich sorcery/witchcraft beliefs, and vice versa; (ii) belief in occult harm should co-occur with occult healing/protection practices; (iii) a rough correspondence between sorcerous threats and the protective measures shamans offer; (iv) shamans should play an outsized role in transmitting occult lore relative to other community members; (v) lower social salience of transmitters should correspond to more muted agential views of misfortune; (vi) agential view of misfortune without background animism would be anomalous and require special explanation.
