# Norenzayan, Shariff, Gervais, Willard, McNamara, Slingerland and Henrich (2016) The Cultural Evolution of Prosocial Religions

#type/paper #theme/CulturalEvolution #theme/Religion #journal/BBS #theory/cultural_group_selection #theory/prosocial_religions

**Bibliography**: Norenzayan, Ara, Azim F. Shariff, Will M. Gervais, Aiyana K. Willard, Rita A. McNamara, Edward Slingerland, and Joseph Henrich. 2016. "The Cultural Evolution of Prosocial Religions." *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 39:e1. doi:10.1017/S0140525X14001356.

## Topic

Develops a cultural evolutionary theory of the origins of prosocial religions and applies it to two linked puzzles in human history: (1) the rise of large-scale cooperation among strangers, and (2) the worldwide spread of prosocial religions over the last 10–12 millennia. The authors argue these developments were mutually energizing — a culturally evolved package of beliefs and practices (moralizing supernatural agents, credible displays of faith, solidarity-building rituals) promoted high fertility and large-scale cooperation with co-religionists, contributing to success in intergroup competition; successful groups then spread these elements through expansion and imitation. The paper is a BBS target article accompanied by open peer commentary and an authors' response.

## Key Theory / Framework

The synthesis claims to reconcile two influential evolutionary approaches: the **by-product approach** (religious representations arise as non-adaptive by-products of ordinary cognition) and the **adaptationist approach** (costly signaling; supernatural punishment hypothesis). The authors begin from the by-product premise but argue that only some cultural variants spread, because of their effects on success in intergroup competition. The framework rests on four conceptual foundations:

> **(1) Reliably developing cognitive biases.** Mentalizing/theory of mind, teleological thinking, and mind–body dualism reliably develop across populations and supply the raw material for supernatural-agent beliefs. These are not unique to religion and need not all be harnessed.

> **(2) Social instincts and third-party monitoring.** Humans evolved sensitivity to cues of social monitoring (e.g., the "watching eyes" effect). The suggestion of morally concerned supernatural watchers with greater surveillance capacity can expand norm compliance beyond that produced by human watchers.

> **(3) Cultural learning and the origins of faith.** Learners rely on content-based mechanisms (minimally counterintuitive concepts), context-based/model-based biases (prestige, success, self-similarity; Henrich & Gil-White 2001), and **credibility-enhancing displays (CREDs)** — sensitivity to deeds a model would not perform unless beliefs were genuine (Henrich 2009). CREDs address the "Zeus Problem": why identical god-concepts command commitment in one time/place and become fiction in another.

> **(4) Cultural group selection.** Intergroup competition — through violent conflict, differential migration, biased copying, and differential group extinction — favors complexes of traits that reduce within-group competition, sustain solidarity, and enhance success against rival groups. The conditions under which intergroup competition shapes cultural evolution are much broader than for genetic evolution.

The authors are careful that religious elements are *not* a necessary condition for cooperation; multiple secular pathways (institutions, norms, markets) also produce large-scale cooperation, and causality runs in a feedback loop. The "religious package" is treated as a statistical pattern governed by specific hypotheses rather than a predefined concept with necessary/sufficient features, which the authors argue circumvents definitional debates about "religion."

## Data & Methods

This is a theoretical synthesis and review; the authors conduct no single primary dataset analysis but marshal converging evidence across disciplines:

- **Cross-cultural anthropological databases** cited via secondary sources: Standard Cross-Cultural Sample (SCCS, 167 societies) and Ethnographic Atlas (724 societies), used in cited analyses (Roes & Raymond 2003; Botero et al. 2014; Peoples & Marlowe 2012) showing correlations between Big Gods and societal size/complexity/ecological duress.
- **Behavioral economic games** from cited cross-cultural fieldwork: Henrich et al. (2010a; 2010b) 15-society study (Dictator and Ultimatum Games); kibbutzim common-pool game (Sosis & Ruffle 2003); Afro-Brazilian public-goods game (Soler 2012).
- **A new meta-analysis of religious priming** (Shariff et al., in press): 25 studies / 4,825 participants on prosocial outcomes; a broader analysis of 93 studies / 11,653 participants on the moderating role of prior belief.
- **Historical and archaeological evidence** (Mesoamerica, Mesopotamia, Egypt, China, Greece, Rome) on the coevolution of ritual elaboration, Big Gods, and societal scale.
- **Demographic and economic data** on fertility (Blume 2009; Norris & Inglehart 2004; Kaufmann 2010) and growth (McCleary & Barro 2006, 81-country panel).

The paper uses no new inferential model; its evidentiary strategy is convergence across ethnographic, historical, archaeological, experimental, and demographic sources.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Big Gods are rare in small-scale societies and common in large-scale ones

Quantitative reviews of the anthropological record indicate that powerful moralizing gods appear in <10% of the smallest-scale human societies but become widespread in large-scale societies (Fig. 1, reprinted from Roes & Raymond 2003). Stark (2001) found only 23.9% of 427 preindustrial societies in the Ethnographic Atlas possessed a god active in human affairs and supportive of morality. The gods of foraging societies (e.g., the Kalahari Gao!na, per Marshall 1962) typically have limited powers and little concern with how community members treat each other. Multiple statistically independent predictors of Big Gods are reported from cited work: society size, agricultural subsistence, animal husbandry (Peoples & Marlowe 2012), and exposure to ecological duress such as water scarcity (Botero et al. 2014; Snarey 1996). The authors stress the data impose a dichotomy but their theory treats the underlying space as a continuum.

### Finding 2: Big Gods and routinized rituals coevolved with societal complexity

Archaeological evidence from Mesoamerica shows rituals became more formal, elaborate, and costly as societies developed from foraging bands (informal, unscheduled rituals before 4000 BP) through multivillage chiefdoms to states with full-time priests, religious calendars, and costly temples (after 2500 BP in Mexico; Marcus & Flannery 2004). Parallel patterns are cited for Mesopotamia, India, predynastic Egypt, and Bronze Age China. Historically, the Abrahamic god is documented evolving from a "limited, whimsical, tribal war god" into a supreme moralizing deity (Wright 2009); early Chinese records show the "Lord on High"/Heaven (shangdi/tian) as a Big God whose Mandate was tied to moral behavior by ~1000 BCE. Using the Human Relations Area Files, Atkinson and Whitehouse (2011) found high-frequency, low-arousal "doctrinal" rituals associated with greater belief in Big Gods, agriculture, and complexity.

### Finding 3: The "Axial Age" framing is rejected as historically misleading

The authors argue against the view (associated with Jaspers 1953 and echoed by Baumard & Boyer 2013) of a sharp chasm between pre–Axial amoral religions and Axial moral religions. Three objections: (a) it ignores the gradual nature of cultural evolution, since pre-Axial chiefdoms and early states already had interventionist, partly moralizing deities; (b) the proposed period (800–200 BCE) is too long and the transition to prosocial religions occurs at very different times globally (e.g., Islam begins in the 6th century CE, ~800 years after the Axial Age closes); and (c) ample evidence (Egyptian Maat; the Coffin Texts; Hammurabi's code) shows "pre-Axial" religions already supported public morality.

### Finding 4: Religious involvement correlates with prosociality, conditional on context

Across a 15-society sample, Henrich et al. (2010a; 2010b) found participation in world religions (Christianity or Islam), relative to local religions, predicted more prosocial play in Dictator and Ultimatum Games, controlling for community size, market integration, income, education, and wealth. Effects of Big-God commitment are strongest when there is no credible threat of earthly third-party punishment, and disappear when a secular third-party punisher is introduced (Laurin et al. 2012b; Henrich et al. 2010a). Comparable cooperative effects appear among religious kibbutzim (Sosis & Ruffle 2003) and an Afro-Brazilian religious group (Soler 2012). Crucially, the association typically fails to appear in WEIRD undergraduate samples, which the authors attribute to strong secular institutions crowding out religion's effect — a conditional pattern, not a universal one.

### Finding 5: Religious priming experimentally increases prosocial behavior, moderated by prior belief

A meta-analysis of 25 religious-priming studies (4,825 participants; Shariff et al., in press) found religious reminders reliably increase prosocial behavior, average Hedges' *g* = 0.27 (95% CI: 0.15 to 0.40; Fig. 2), an effect that remains robust though reduced after adjusting for publication bias. A broader analysis (93 studies, 11,653 participants) showed the effect is moderated by prior religious belief: reliable for strong believers, vanishing for nonbelievers, indicating that exogenous primes interact with endogenous belief rather than reflecting low-level associations.

### Finding 6: Supernatural punishment and benevolence have divergent effects

Greater belief that God is *punishing* is more strongly associated with reduced cheating, whereas greater belief that God is *benevolent* tends, if anything, to increase cheating (Shariff & Norenzayan 2011). At the national level, greater belief in hell relative to heaven predicts lower crime rates (e.g., burglary), holding socioeconomic factors and dominant denomination constant (Shariff & Rhemtulla 2012). Reaction-time work suggests believers intuit that God knows about norm-violating behaviors specifically (Purzycki et al. 2012), and belief in a punishing god is associated with *less* personal punishment of free-riders, because punishment is offloaded to God (Laurin et al. 2012b). Prosocial religions are also argued to cultivate self-control: religious primes increased willingness to endure unpleasant experiences and delay gratification (Rounding et al. 2012; Laurin et al. 2012a).

### Finding 7: CREDs, synchrony, fictive kinship, and sacred values galvanize solidarity

Extravagant displays (painful initiations, fasts, celibacy, vows of poverty) are explained as CREDs that deepen faith because they would be unlikely if the model's belief were not genuine; religious leaders' self-sacrifice (Cybele priests' self-castration; Christian martyrs) sparked cultural epidemics of belief (Burkert 1982; Stark 1996), and in Scandinavia children adopt parents' religion less when parents display no CREDs (Lanman 2012). Witnessing the extreme Kavadi ritual increased anonymous temple donations as much as participating did, with donation size correlating with perceived pain (Xygalatas et al. 2013). Synchronous arousal ("collective effervescence," Durkheim 1915) increases affiliation, trust, and cooperation (Wiltermuth & Heath 2009). The authors also argue cultural evolution anchors group-beneficial norms to metaphysical bedrock as "sacred values" resistant to cost–benefit trade-offs; normative monogamy is offered as a case where Christianity made monogamy divinely ordained, overriding elite male interests, with group-beneficial effects such as reduced crime (Henrich et al. 2012). All of these mechanisms are emphasized as non-unique to religion (synchrony in military drill, fictive kinship in small-scale societies, sacred values among secular atheists).

### Finding 8: Cultural group selection of religious groups operates through differential survival, conversion, reproduction, and economic success

Ethnohistorical cases document belief–ritual complexes spreading via intergroup competition: New Guinea highlands ideological systems (Wiessner & Tumu 1998); the Ilahita Arapesh Tambara ritual system permitting 1,500 people to cohere where villages typically fission above ~300, imitated from the expanding Abelam (Tuzin 1976; 2001); and the spread of Islam among the Orma via CREDs that built trust for trade (Ensminger 1997). Cross-cultural analysis finds intergroup warfare (not within-group violence) predicts more extreme male rites (Sosis et al. 2007). Quantitatively, 19th-century American religious communes outlasted secular ones by an average factor of four over a 120-year span (Sosis 2000; Fig. 3), and number of imposed restrictions predicted commune longevity (R² = 0.38), controlling for population size, income, and founding year (Sosis & Bressler 2003). Prosocial religions are also pro-natalist — weekly-or-more worshippers averaged 2.5 children vs 1.7 for those who never worship across 82 countries — and afterlife belief predicts faster economic growth in an 81-country panel (McCleary & Barro 2006).

### Finding 9: The framework reframes the supernatural punishment hypothesis and accounts for secularization and atheism

The authors argue their cultural-selection account preserves the insights of costly signaling and the supernatural punishment hypothesis (SPH) while resolving anomalies: against the SPH's genetic-adaptation claim, they note that forager gods have limited omniscience and little moral concern, and that gods become *more* moralizing precisely as societies scale up and anonymity makes escaping social sanctions easier, not harder. They propose "God and government are psychologically interchangeable": secular authority primes affect generosity as much as God primes (Shariff & Norenzayan 2007), and lowering faith in one control system raises faith in the other (Kay et al. 2008). Secularization is explained as societies "climbing the ladder of prosocial religion and then kicking it away" once strong secular institutions provide existential security (Norris & Inglehart 2004). Atheism is treated not as one phenomenon but as several pathways: mindblind atheism (mentalizing deficits), InCREDulous atheism (absence of witnessed CREDs), apatheism (absence of existential threat), and analytic atheism (analytic override of intuition).
