# Zahavi (1975) Mate Selection—A Selection for a Handicap

#type/paper #theme/CostlySignaling #theme/Cooperation #journal/JTB #theory/handicap_principle #theory/costly_signaling

**Bibliography**: Zahavi, Amotz. 1975. "Mate Selection—A Selection for a Handicap." *Journal of Theoretical Biology* 53:205–214. (Received 23 July 1974, in revised form 2 December 1974. Institute for Nature Conservation Research, Faculty of Life Sciences, The George S. Wise Centre for Life Sciences, Tel-Aviv University, Israel.)

## Topic

A theoretical paper proposing that characters which develop through mate preference confer handicaps on the selected individuals in their survival, and that these handicaps are useful to the selecting sex precisely because they test the quality of the mate. The puzzle Zahavi addresses is the one Darwin (1874) left open: it is commonly observed that the most beautiful males of a bird species, or the deer with the largest antlers, are preferred by females, yet there is no simple explanation of in what way the preferred males should be of better quality than others. Darwin assumed female preference rather than explaining it. Zahavi argues that the size of characters selected in this way serves as a mark of quality, and that this interpretation may provide an alternative to hypotheses invoking complicated selective mechanisms — such as group selection or kin selection — which do not act directly on the individual.

## Key Theory / Framework

Zahavi positions the paper against the prior explanatory options for female preference.

**Darwin (1874)** suggested that disadvantages to male survival induced by sexually selected characters are compensated for by more or better females preferring that individual. But he could not satisfactorily explain why females should prefer certain males; he simply assumed they prefer certain male types. **Wallace (1889)** therefore dismissed the theory of sexual selection by mate preference altogether, while **Poulton (1890)** defended it.

**Fisher (1930)** suggested there was initially a correlation between the character preferred by the female and the quality of the male; once sexual selection begins to have its effect, it generates a rapid chain of events in which the preference itself creates a selective pressure accounting for the exaggerated development of the character. At the end of the process, males with exaggerated characters are attractive not necessarily because they are better than they were earlier, but because they and their sons are more attractive to females.

Zahavi's objection is twofold. First, he notes that Fisher's runaway argument contrasts with Fisher's own statement that "tastes of organisms like other organs and faculties must be regarded as the products of evolutionary change governed by the relative advantage which such tastes may confer." Second, he attacks the single-character assumption:

> O'Donald's model (1962), in line with Fisher's argument, assumed that females evaluate males through a single character. With only one measurement at hand, an exaggeration of the character beyond its significance as an indicator of quality will be undetected by females. But under most circumstances females probably select males by more than one character — voice, colour, movement, form. An exaggeration of only one of these characters, without correlation to the quality of the males, should lose its effect by a negative selection: any female which continues to prefer such a character will end up with a worse mate than females which choose males by all other characters except the exaggerated one.

He therefore concludes that generally it should be assumed that a character in a male is attractive to a female *because it helps the female to select the better male*. He recruits **Maynard Smith (1958)** ("sexual selection will have evolutionary consequences only if those individuals which have characteristics which make them successful, in the competitions for a mate, are also fitter than the average as parents") and **Williams (1966)** (mate selection as an "evolutionary battle of the sexes" which "fosters a skilled salesmanship among the males and an equally well-developed sales resistance in the females"), remarking that it is difficult to see how either could agree with Fisher that a state of comparative stability may be reached in which male attractiveness is the main reason males are preferred.

Zahavi credits **Emlen (1973)** with having given an argument along the same line, among several other arguments.

## Data & Methods

Purely theoretical. There is no original data collection, no dataset, no sample size, and no statistical method. The argument is verbal and graphical (Fig. 1), supported by a discussion of a sample of characters drawn largely from ornithology (bird plumage dimorphism, peacock plumes, lek displays, kittiwake and gannet colony behaviour, territory size, sea bird plumage) and by cited work from other authors.

Zahavi is explicit about the epistemic status of this evidence. He states that because the effects of natural and sexual selection are often mixed from the beginning — sexual selection preferably acts on characters already correlated with quality, and natural selection can also affect characters which evolved by sexual selection if these gain some new function — it is almost impossible to separate the two cleanly, and "a discussion of the effect of sexual selection will therefore have to be speculative." Of the examples he writes: "The cases are not presented as a proof of the handicap principle. My aim is to call the attention to the possibility that the value of many characters may reside in their action as testing devices." He notes that the evolution of all the characters discussed may be explained by more than one hypothesis, and that determining which alternative is true calls for detailed examination of the facts and for further experimental confirmation.

## Key Findings

### The core claim: sexual selection works by making quality detectable

Zahavi's proposal is that sexual selection is effective because it improves the ability of the selecting sex to detect quality in the selected sex. The selecting sex benefits because it can be assured of the quality of its mate; the selected sex benefits because it can better advertise its quality and thus probably acquires more or a better mate. But both sexes also lose. Males lose by investing time, energy, and risk in advertising. Females may receive less help from their mates and bear sons which are less fit to stand the pressure of natural selection, since those sons also carry the genotype which invests more in attracting females.

### Why the handicap must lower survival: the equilibrium argument

A survey of characters probably selected through mate preference makes it clear that probably all of them confer a handicap on survival, and Zahavi argues the handicap could also be derived as a logical conclusion. Before mate selection achieved its evolutionary effect, the organism was in equilibrium with the pressures of natural selection. If the selective pressure of mate preference — which has no value to the survival of the individual — is added to the existing selective pressures, the effect must be negative. The larger the effect of the preference, the more developed the character and the larger the handicap imposed. Hence a character affected by sexual selection should be correlated to the handicap it imposes.

This is where Zahavi separates himself from his predecessors. The antagonism between natural and sexual selection had already been mentioned by Darwin and Fisher, but they saw the antagonism as a *by-product* of the mechanism. Zahavi's interpretation implies that **sexual selection is effective only by selecting a character which lowers the survival of the individual**.

### The handicap as a test, and the compromise the female accepts

The handicap can be considered a kind of test imposed on the individual. An individual with a well-developed sexually selected character is an individual which has survived a test; a female which can discriminate a male possessing the character from one without it can discriminate between a male which has passed a test and one which has not been tested. The more developed the character, the more severe was the test, so females which select males with the most developed characters can be sure they have selected from among the best genotypes of the male population (Fig. 1).

Zahavi is careful about what the female gives up. An individual with a very good genotype but without a handicap is certainly fitter than a handicapped individual which otherwise possesses the same genotype — but because an individual without the handicapping marker does not advertise its quality, a potential mate cannot spot it. Females which choose by a sexually selected character therefore *compromise*: they select a good quality male which is handicapped, but they can be assured as to their mate's quality. Sexual selection proceeds in producing an effect only as long as females benefit more from the assurance of their mates' quality than they lose by mating with a handicapped mate, and as long as the males can survive the handicap. Males which do not invest in parental care can spend more in order to pass the test of quality (Trivers, 1972; Selander, 1972).

Fig. 1 plots quality of the mate on the vertical axis against magnitude of the sexually selected character (the handicap) on the horizontal. At the intersection of the axes the character is of the size which best fits the selection pressure of natural selection. An increase in the character, and hence in the handicap, eliminates low quality individuals. The selecting sex cannot distinguish quality among potential mates with an equally developed attractive character. The more developed the attractive character, the higher the average quality of a potential mate.

### No genetic linkage required: the handicap excludes bluff

This is the argument that distinguishes the model most sharply from indicator models. In Zahavi's model there is **no need to assume any special genetic linkage** between the marker of quality and the quality of the individual, because whenever the marker is present in a phenotype, the handicap it has already imposed is itself proof that the phenotype — and hence very likely also the genotype — is above a certain level of quality (the lower curve in Fig. 1).

The positive case for this is anti-bluff: since any genetic linkage between a marker and quality is open to change and hence to bluff, i.e. low quality individuals may be tagged by a high quality marker, the test of the handicap is a simple way to exclude bluff.

### When handicaps do not evolve

The effects of sexual selection should vary, as Darwin suggested, and have to be "compatible with the existence of the species." Some species, and often the sex which invests more in parental care, cannot easily be pushed into a handicap because the pressures of natural selection are too tight. In those cases mate selection might be achieved either through an easier test with a smaller handicap, or without any test at all — and in the last case a mate may be selected but "sexual selection" cannot operate. Under such circumstances mate selection is probably less reliable and takes more time.

Zahavi also notes that although the female is usually referred to as the selecting sex and the male as the selected sex, both sexes may select, and sexual selection may take effect on both sexes, as in some monomorphic species. Sometimes the female is the more affected sex (Selander, 1972; Trivers, 1972).

### Illustrative case: dimorphic vs. monomorphic plumage, and northern ducks

The accepted explanation for cryptic females and colourful males is that females cannot withstand the extra predation pressure of colourful plumage since they must attend more to the nest. But Zahavi points out that there are as many species in which both sexes are cryptic, and males of such species also mate and fight rivals. The interesting question is why some species should "find it better" to be excited or intimidated by the more colourful males, which only makes sense if colourful males are of better quality. A mature colourful male has already proved itself of better quality than a cryptic one because it has already withstood the extra predation risk of its plumage. Quality is probably more difficult to discriminate among cryptic males; another way to evaluate a cryptic male is to observe it over long periods. This may explain Lein's (1973) finding that monomorphic species of warblers take longer to pair than sexually dimorphic species with colourful male plumage, and may be why northern duck species, whose short breeding season demands a short period of pair formation, evolved striking male plumage.

Against **Mayr's (1972)** suggestion that the striking male pattern of drakes reduces interspecies hybridization — so that populations not facing hybridization risk lose their bright male plumage — Zahavi asks why duck species did not instead evolve other isolating mechanisms less risky than bright plumage. His alternative is that bright male colouration evolved first as an indicator of drake quality, and, varying geographically with environmental selective pressures, eliminated hybridization to the extent that other isolating mechanisms were not necessary and hence could not evolve.

### Illustrative case: peacock plumes and the riskiness of displays

The excessive tail plumes of the peacock are obviously deleterious to survival: the more brilliant the plumes the more conspicuous the male to predators, and the longer the plumes the more difficult it may be to escape predators or to move about in everyday activity. Only the best males would be able to sustain the handicap, so if females select males handicapped by long plumes, they select for quality. Zahavi notes it would certainly be better for females to choose high quality males which were *not* handicapped by the plumes, and therefore "we have to assume that a discrimination for quality is more difficult without the test of the plumes."

Many, if not all, sexual displays endanger their performers, and many seem designed specifically for that purpose: warblers sing out of cover, lek species dance daily in the open in the same places. The extra risk cannot be merely to communicate the whereabouts of a male ready to mate. Zahavi's test of this is comparative: if displays had evolved to communicate whereabouts in the most efficient way, saving energy and reducing predation hazard as many alarm calls have (Marler, 1955), they would not serve as markers of quality.

### Illustrative case: fights for central colony sites

Coulson (1968) found that kittiwakes which breed in the centre of the colony are of better quality, as judged by their reproductive performance, than birds breeding in the periphery. Patterson (1964) found that centrally located territories in the black-headed gull are more protected from predators, so that the fight for central territories could there be explained as a consequence of predation pressure. Zahavi observes that kittiwakes nest on cliffs and are comparatively free of predation, that many well-protected sites in the colony are vacant while the ferocious fight for the central territories goes on, and that fights for central sites are also found in large sea birds such as gannets, which nest on islands free from predators. An initial tendency to breed together, possibly in order to get information about food (Ward & Zahavi, 1973), may give rise to some fights for centrally located sites; such fights, however small, may further evolve through mate preference to serve as an index of quality. The method is a meaningful test because the ability of colonial birds to fight one another for food is important, and only better quality birds can depart from offshore feeding grounds early in the season and succeed in occupying the central places. Central birds handicap themselves in time and energy but are compensated by better quality mates.

### Illustrative case: territory size as an alternative to group selection

Males often claim territories much larger than they appear to need, and the selective agent for large territory size is not easy to see. Wynne-Edwards (1962) suggested the adaptive significance is to regulate food supply to population size, insuring the persistence of food supplies for future generations — altruistic behaviour which calls for group selection theory. Zahavi's alternative is mate selection: males occupying small territories seem to be less successful in acquiring a mate (Watson, pers. comm.; Zahavi, unpublished observation on *Oenanthe*); since a small territory probably cannot easily support a family, a tendency to select a mate occupying a large territory may increase through mate preference to the point where males occupy territories much larger than their immediate needs. A fight for a larger territory must be more difficult than a fight for a small one, so males which succeed in occupying large territories should be of better quality, and females which select such males also select for quality. **The conservation of food supply for the population may be a side effect rather than the causal mechanism which selects for territory size.** Zahavi notes O'Donald (1963) had already suggested sexual selection may be involved in territorial behaviour, but understood the selective mechanism somewhat differently.

### Illustrative case: "altruistic" plumage as an alternative to reciprocal altruism

The contrasting black and white plumage displayed by many adult sea birds, especially when flying and diving, serves in communication since it attracts more individuals to food sources. The evolution and retention of this bright "altruistic" plumage is difficult to explain: in a predominantly altruistic population, any individual with "selfish" dull plumage may do better, since it benefits from sources of food found by all other birds while not having to share the food it finds itself. Zahavi's indication that mate preference may be involved is developmental: only adult birds have the "altruistic" plumage, while immature birds, even at two years old, have "selfish" plumage. The advantage of selecting a mate with altruistic plumage is that it selects an individual which has already passed the test, and the test is meaningful because birds breeding in colonies have to compete daily over food.

He explicitly contrasts this with the alternative explanation along the lines of reciprocal altruism (Trivers, 1971), which calls for a mechanism of punishments to guard against selfish individuals: "if mate preference is responsible for the selection of the altruistic character then no punishment is needed and 'altruism' is compensated for by a better quality mate."

Generalizing, Zahavi expects mate preference in all sexually reproducing organisms and hence, if natural selection allows, the evolution of markers for the differentiation of quality. He notes O'Donald (1963) suggested altruistic behaviour may function as a marker for quality selected through mate preference, and that the idea was not followed, perhaps because it was believed that the evolution of exaggerations through mate preference is only a by-product of a "run-away" process (Fisher, 1930; O'Donald, 1962). When the handicap is considered the key to the selection process rather than its by-product, it is reasonable to expect handicaps — and consequently also altruistic behaviour — to be widespread phenomena.

### Refinement: the handicap should be in a character important to the selecting sex

Zahavi distinguishes his position from Emlen's (1973), who supported some of the arguments but also accepted the possibility of random fixation of such characters, and suggested that the importance of the character lies in the animal's ability to waste energy to show off its prowess, with the particular means often accidentally determined.

Against this, the handicap principle as Zahavi understands it suggests that the marker of quality should evolve to handicap the selected sex **in a character which is important to the selecting sex**, since the selecting sex tests, through the handicap, the quality of its potential mate in characters which are of importance. Hence the attracting character should be related to the special ecological problems of the species, and its adaptive significance should lower the fitness of the selected sex in relation to those main ecological problems. The selecting sex should be attracted by a marker only when the handicap it imposes on its mate and its offspring is smaller than the advantage gained by securing a better (tested) mate. Zahavi adds that this evolutionary mechanism need not differ in its rate from any other selection process, and that attracting characters should evolve only as a consequence of an environmental change which requires a different kind of test.

### Characters which attract mates vs. characters which intimidate rivals

Characters which attract the other sex cannot always be separated from those which intimidate rivals of the same sex, but Zahavi holds that the effects of mate preference should be much larger than those resulting from intrasexual conflicts, deferring the reasons to a later publication. He notes Fisher (1930) came to the same conclusion in stating that male ornaments acquired through male–male conflict may be striking but could scarcely ever become as extravagant as characters which develop to impress a potential mate.
