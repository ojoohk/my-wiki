# Henrich (2009) The evolution of costly displays, cooperation and religion: credibility enhancing displays and their implications for cultural evolution

#type/paper #theme/Ritual #theme/CulturalEvolution #journal/EHB #theory/credibility_enhancing_displays #theory/cultural_group_selection

**Bibliography**: Henrich, Joseph. 2009. "The Evolution of Costly Displays, Cooperation and Religion: Credibility Enhancing Displays and Their Implications for Cultural Evolution." *Evolution and Human Behavior* 30(4):244–260.

## Topic

This paper introduces credibility enhancing displays (CREDs) as an evolutionary theory for why apparently costly ritual acts — firewalking, ritual scarification, animal sacrifice, martyrdom — become cognitively salient and culturally stable. Henrich argues that the evolution of cheap symbolic communication created an adaptive problem (exploitation by manipulators who misrepresent their beliefs), which natural selection addressed by shaping learners to attend to a model's actions as evidence of genuine belief commitment. The paper proceeds in three parts: (I) the evolution of the CRED cognitive bias; (II) a formal cultural evolutionary model showing CREDs sustain costly practice–belief combinations; (III) Cultural Group Selection (CGS) as the mechanism favoring costly acts that promote in-group cooperation.

## Key Theory / Framework

**Credibility Enhancing Displays (CREDs)**: The core proposition is that cultural learners face a Machiavellian manipulation problem once cheap symbolic (verbal) communication evolves. Natural selection favored a learning bias: attending to displays or actions by a model that would be costly to perform if the model held a belief different from what she expressed verbally. Such actions — CREDs — provide the learner with a reliable measure of the model's degree of commitment or belief. CREDs need not be costly in absolute terms; what matters is that they are less likely to be performed by someone who does not hold the expressed belief. Costly acts constitute particularly powerful CREDs because the cost is especially credibility-enhancing.

**Formal Cultural Evolutionary Model (replicator dynamics)**: Henrich models the co-evolution of a belief (θ, dichotomous: 0 or 1) and a practice (x, dichotomous: 0 or 1). Neither belief variant has an independent transmission advantage; the practice x=1 incurs cost c on individual success. The belief θ=1 creates a content bias b for acquiring practice x=1. The key cognitive parameters are: σ (generalized skepticism toward cheaply expressed beliefs), ψ (effect of x=1 in reducing skepticism toward the paired θ=1), and δ (effect of x=0 in reducing skepticism toward θ=0). The model uses ϕ (frequency of θ=1 believers) and q (frequency of x=1 practitioners) with replicator dynamics to derive conditions for stable equilibria.

**Cultural Group Selection (CGS)**: Part III extends the argument. Because the formal model reveals multiple stable equilibria — including ones with costly practices — competition among groups stabilized at different equilibria can favor the spread of belief–practice combinations that increase within-group cooperation and intergroup competitiveness. CGS can occur through (1) military/economic/technological defeat of less cooperative groups; (2) demographic competition (more culture bearers); (3) prestige-biased imitation of more successful groups' beliefs and practices.

## Data & Methods

This is a theoretical paper; no original empirical data are reported. Two analytical components:

1. **Formal model**: replicator dynamics; two recursion equations for Δϕ and Δq per time step; learners encounter one model per step and update representations in proportion to the difference between learner and model weightings. Model weightings for belief transmission are modified by c, σ, ψ, and δ (see Table 1 in paper). Model weightings for practice transmission are modified by c and b. β normalizes weights to probabilities.

2. **Review of psychological literature**: Henrich reviews experimental findings from four domains to support the claim that CRED-consistent patterns exist in human cultural learning: food preferences, opinion transmission, altruism, and beliefs in intangible entities. All cited studies are others' work.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Three stable equilibrium situations for belief–practice coevolution

The formal model yields three relevant stable situations. (1) *No-cost state*: only one stable equilibrium at ϕ=q=0 (everyone holds θ=0, no one performs x=1); this arises if b≤c or if ψ=δ=0. Without CREDs or without content bias exceeding cost, costly practices do not stabilize — replicating existing work. (2) *Two simultaneously stable equilibria*: (0,0) and (1,1) arise when Condition (3) is satisfied (frequency of θ=1 believers exceeds c/b) and Condition (4) is satisfied (frequency of x=1 practitioners exceeds a critical threshold derived from ψ/δ and c); both ψ>0 and δ>0 are required. Henrich illustrates this with parameter values b=0.4, c=0.2, ψ=0.1, δ=0.05, β=0.2; the unstable internal equilibrium is calculated as qt=0.385, ϕt=0.5 (see Fig. 1B). (3) *Only the costly equilibrium (ϕ=q=1) is stable*: if ψ>δ, δ=0, and b>c; the authors treat this as a specialized limiting case. Situation 2 — multiple stable equilibria — is identified as the theoretically central case.

### Finding 2: CRED bias evident in food preference transmission

Harper and Sanders (1975), as reviewed by Henrich, conducted an experiment in which a female experimenter visited homes of children (ages 14 to 48 months) and presented a novel food. In the baseline condition (declarative statement only), 25% of children tasted the food. In the CRED condition (experimenter stated the same thing while also sampling the food), 75% of children tasted it (p<.05). Henrich interprets this as a manifestation of learners attending to whether a model's action is consistent with her verbal expression.

### Finding 3: CRED effects in opinion transmission via counter-self-interest expressions

Walster, Aronson, and Abrahams (1966), as reviewed, had subjects read newspaper articles in which a high-prestige (famed prosecutor) or low-prestige (thug) individual argued for changes in criminal justice — positions either consistent with or contrary to the model's self-interest. Subjects' opinions shifted substantially more toward a model who argued against his own interests. Furthermore, a low-prestige model arguing counter to self-interest produced more opinion change than a high-prestige model arguing for his own interest. Henrich interprets the counter-self-interest verbal expression itself as a CRED: it would be unlikely to be disseminated if the model actually held a self-serving view.

### Finding 4: Cultural transmission of altruism requires a CRED (actual donation)

Developmental experiments (Bryan 1971; Bryan & Walbek 1970a, 1970b; and others), as reviewed, used a bowling-game paradigm in which a model either did or did not donate tokens to a charity jar and either preached or did not preach generosity. Preaching alone produced little or no effect on children's donation behavior. When the model actually donated, subjects donated more generously. Verbal exhortations combined with actual donation allowed learners to generalize altruism to broader contexts (Grusec, Saas-Kortsaak, & Simutis, 1978). Henrich interprets actual donation as a CRED: it would be unlikely if the model did not hold the expressed pro-donation preference.

### Finding 5: Beliefs in intangible entities (God, germs) require behavioral endorsement (CREDs)

Harris and Koenig (2006) and Harris et al. (2006), as reviewed, show that children's belief in intangible entities depends on whether adults' behavior "endorses" those entities. Adults pray to God, attend rituals, and instruct prayer — CREDs for God. Adults refuse dropped food and require handwashing while expressing concern for germs — CREDs for germs. Entities that do not inspire adult CREDs (mermaids) are not strongly believed by children. Henrich argues this is consistent with the claim that cultural learning is sensitive to CREDs in committing to counterintuitive representations.

### Finding 6: Costly requirements predict longevity of religious (not secular) communes

Sosis and Bressler (2003), as reviewed, assembled data on 83 utopian communities in the 19th century. The number of costly requirements (restrictions on food, sex, material possessions, marriage, parenting rights) strongly predicts longevity of religious communes; this effect does not emerge for secular communes. Henrich argues this is a direct example of CGS: differential survival of groups ratchets up the mean number of costly requirements over time.

### Finding 7: Ritual attendance predicts cooperation in religious (not secular) kibbutzim

Sosis and Ruffle (2003, 2004), as reviewed, used a one-shot dyadic contribution game (common-pot structure: contributions increase 50% and split equally) among secular and religious members of Israeli kibbutzim. Greater attendance at public rituals predicts higher contributions among members of religious kibbutzim (controlling for other factors); effect not found in secular kibbutzim. High ritual attenders in religious kibbutzim also contributed substantially more when paired with fellow kibbutz members than when paired with non-member Israelis; secular kibbutz members showed no such differential.

### Finding 8: Ritual participation predicts support for intergroup aggression independent of prayer

Ginges, Hansen, and Norenzayan (2007), as reviewed, used survey and experimental data from Palestinians and Jewish Israelis, and representative samples of Indonesian Muslims, Mexican Catholics, British Protestants, Russian Orthodox, Jewish Israelis, and Indian Hindus. Greater ritual attendance independently predicts (1) support for suicide bomber attacks against out-groups; (2) willingness to die for one's god; and (3) blaming other religions for much of the world's troubles — all controlling for prayer frequency and other factors.
