# Singh (2018) The Cultural Evolution of Shamanism

#type/paper #theme/CulturalEvolution #theme/Religion #journal/BBS #theory/cultural_evolutionary_theory_of_shamanism

**Bibliography**: Singh, Manvir. 2018. "The Cultural Evolution of Shamanism." *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* 41:e66. doi:10.1017/S0140525X17001893.

## Topic

Proposes a cultural evolutionary theory of why shamanism — defined as practices by trance-using practitioners with jurisdiction over uncertain outcomes — consistently develops across human societies. Addresses three puzzles: why shamanic traditions exhibit recurrent features worldwide; why shamanism professionalizes early, often in the absence of other specialization; and how shifting social conditions affect the form or existence of shamanism. The paper is a BBS target article accompanied by 26 open peer commentaries.

## Key Theory / Framework

The theory holds that shamanism is a suite of practices developed through cultural evolution that adapts to innate human cognitive dispositions to convince observers that a practitioner can influence otherwise uncontrollable outcomes. Two foundational bodies of research are invoked: (1) cognitive science of religion — research on error management, mentalizing, causal cognition, and folk conceptions of humanness (Boyer 2001; Atran & Henrich 2010); and (2) cultural evolutionary theory — the differential transmission and adoption of cultural variants driven by individuals' adoption strategies (Boyd & Richerson 1985; Henrich 2015).

The theory has four linked components:

> **Psychology of superstition**: Individuals adopt causally innocuous interventions ("superstitions") when outcomes are uncontrollable, fitness-relevant, and random. Individuals are most likely to adopt superstitions when (1) the potential benefit is high and (2) the intervention is followed by success some proportion of the time. Contexts of greatest uncertainty attract the most superstitious thinking.

> **Cultural selection of plausible-seeming interventions**: Because superstitions cannot be validated by outcome, individuals retain those that *seem* most effective given their intuitions about causality. This drives cultural evolution of practices adapted to our intuitions of efficacy (e.g., Legare & Souza 2012 showing multi-step, time-specific rituals are rated as more efficacious). Competition among shamans accelerates this selection.

> **Attribution of uncertain outcomes to invisible agents**: People explain uncontrollable events by invoking invisible, agentic forces (gods, spirits, witchcraft, fate). This tendency is cross-culturally universal and is attributed to sociocognitive biases (mentalizing, hypervigilant agent detection) and cultural transmission. Consequently, the most plausible-seeming superstitious practitioner is one who claims to interact with those invisible forces.

> **Violating humanness to signal supernatural ability**: People more readily attribute supernatural powers to individuals who deviate from folk biology or psychology. The proposed theory posits that trance is a "drama of strangeness" — behavioral displays that violate notions of ordinary human functioning — and that initiation practices (death and rebirth, surgery, bodily insertions, asceticism) serve to convince community members that the practitioner has stably transformed into a non-human entity. This is explicitly *not* a cooperative costly signaling account: transformative practices do not indicate commitment to stay in a group or adherence to cooperative beliefs, and in many societies they are associated with malicious sorcery, not cooperation.

The theory is agnostic about whether shamans provide benefits; it posits only that practices develop to promote the *perception* of efficacy.

## Data & Methods

This is a theoretical synthesis paper; Singh conducts no primary data collection. Secondary analyses are conducted on:

- **Winkelman & White (1987) cross-cultural database**: 115 magico-religious practitioners across 45 societies; 75 practitioners in 43 societies used trance to provide services (qualifying as shamans for this paper's purposes). Singh calculated jurisdiction frequencies from this dataset. To distinguish politically simple from complex societies, this database was merged with the Ethnographic Atlas (Gray 1999), using EA variable 33 (Jurisdictional hierarchy of local community).
- **Peoples et al. (2016) review of hunter-gatherer religion**: Shamans identified in 29 of 33 hunter-gatherer societies surveyed.
- **Winkelman (1986a; 1986b; 1990) SCCS-based analyses**: Trance practitioners found in 43 of 47 societies in a modified Standard Cross-Cultural Sample subsample.

Additional evidence is drawn from extensive ethnographic case material and experimental psychology literature. The paper does not use inferential statistics or regression models.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Shamanism is near-universal in documented human societies

Shamans appear in 29 of 33 hunter-gatherer societies examined by Peoples et al. (2016); of the four exceptions, one (Siriono) likely had shamans recently and another (Mbuti) had members who visited nearby "witch doctors." Winkelman (1986a; 1986b; 1990) found trance practitioners in 43 of 47 societies in a modified SCCS subsample. Ethnographic and archaeological evidence suggests shamanism characterized many ancestral human societies.

### Finding 2: The shaman's jurisdiction concentrates on uncertain outcomes

Using Winkelman & White's (1987) dataset (N=43 societies with trance practitioners), Singh calculated that shamans treated illness in 100% of societies, assisted food acquisition (finding game, crop success) in 91%, controlled weather in 65%, and provided divination in 100%. In contrast, shamans participated in judiciary, military, and economic activities at lower rates, and these roles are argued to be downstream of shamans' supernatural authority rather than independent jurisdictions. The pattern is more pronounced when restricting to politically simple societies.

### Finding 3: Trance is a drama of strangeness, not a universal neurophysiological state

Singh rejects the claims of a cross-culturally consistent "Shamanic State of Consciousness" (Harner 1990) or "integrative mode of consciousness" (Winkelman 2000) on the grounds that different trance inducements (sensory deprivation, hallucinogens, starvation, drumming, relaxation) produce non-analogous physiological and cognitive changes. Instead, trance is proposed to be a performance that violates folk intuitions of humanness. Observers across cultures evaluate trance performances in terms of their apparent strangeness: Monguor shamans were considered inauthentic if they flinched at a whip crack; Japanese communities condemned trance as "weak and unconvincing" when it included polite language or decorous wand-waving. Cultural selection for more effective-seeming performances drives divergence in trance forms. The theatrical nature of trance does not require conscious fakery; cultural selection should favor interventions that change the felt experience of both shaman and client, since physician expectations increase the placebo effect magnitude.

### Finding 4: Trance performances include displays of superhuman ability

In line with the "drama of strangeness" hypothesis, trance performances across societies incorporate exhibits of putative superhuman abilities (Table 1): immunity to fire (Fon, !Kung, Japanese, Mentawai, Tlingit, and others); immunity to other pain and injury (Azande, Koreans, Koryak, Saami, and others); possession of inaccessible knowledge (speaking unknown languages: Evenks, Greeks, Haida, Yakut, and others); and physical impossibilities such as removing one's nose or turning water into blood (Apache, Chukchi, Mapuche, and others).

### Finding 5: Initiation practices indicate biological or psychological transformation

Shamanic initiations frequently involve claims of bodily transformation (Table 2): death and rebirth (Andaman Islanders, Bororo, Inuit, Mapuche, Nuba, and others — death-and-rebirth reported in 9 of 43 societies in Winkelman & White's dataset); magical surgery or dismemberment, especially of eyes, ears, and head (Dayak, Haitians, Igbo, Mentawai, Yanomamö); transmission of magical substances (crystals, dust, phlegm of existing shamans: Aranda, Azande, Kwakwaka'wakw, Maori, Ojibwa, and others); and spontaneous biological anomaly such as serious illness, epilepsy, or frenzy (Buryats, Koreans, Niassans, Nomlaki, Uzbeks). According to Winkelman & White (1987), at least one trance practitioner in 49% of societies observed sex prohibitions (during training or ceremonies), 53% food prohibitions, 72% social isolation, and 83% at least one form of self-denial.

### Finding 6: Transformative practices are not cooperative costly signals

Singh argues against interpreting initiations and asceticism as cooperative costly signals (Irons 2001; Sosis 2004; Bulbulia & Sosis 2011). Four arguments are offered: (a) many taboos recur perennially rather than being offset in time from benefits, so they cannot signal commitment to remain in the group; (b) observing prohibitions does not entail subscribing to cooperative religious beliefs; (c) self-denial is often said to produce abilities for *malicious* activities (Jivaro sorcerers, Barama River Carib kanaima, early modern English black-fasting) and for non-cooperative skills (Canela hunting, Haida war prowess); and (d) emic accounts emphasize transformation and supernaturalization, not cooperativeness.

### Finding 7: Shamanism professionalizes because transformation creates entry requirements

Shamanism becomes the first (and often only) profession in many small-scale societies because individuals must demonstrate biological or psychological transformation to claim the jurisdiction over uncertain outcomes. This creates a stable class with entry requirements. Technical jurisdictions (canoe making, cooking) do not professionalize in small-scale societies because high levels of interaction and the simplicity of physical technology make technical knowledge difficult to conceal and easy for outsiders to replicate after minimal observation. The rapid diffusion of technologies like the domesticated sweet potato and the horse — despite active attempts at control — illustrates this contrast.

### Finding 8: Social conditions modulate entry requirements and shamanism's existence

Competition among shamans pushes investment in credibility-building practices upward. A hypothetical model is presented in which practitioners prefer to invest at i₀ (maximizing benefit minus cost), but competition pushes investment to i₁ (where marginal cost equals benefit). Reduced competition predicts lowered investment: Blacker (1975) observed an unenergetic Japanese possessed shaman who was tolerated only because she was the last miko in the area. Conversely, individuals with inherent deviations from humanness (extra finger, epilepsy, ambiguous sexual identity) pay lower costs for credibility and can thus attain higher investment levels than typical practitioners.

Seven conditions predict shamanism's dissolution: (1) decreased client willingness to pay without corresponding relaxation of entry requirements; (2) demographic fragmentation that destroys complex cultural repertoires (the Siriono case: lost shamanism alongside canoes, games, poison, and firemaking knowledge); (3) powerful competitors controlling the supernatural jurisdiction (as Catholic churches did in late medieval England); (4) reduced uncertainty in everyday life; (5) decline of belief in invisible forces as causes of uncertain outcomes; (6) decline of belief that humans can interact with invisible forces; and (7) naturalization of trance behaviors, so they no longer signal non-humanness (as occurred in 16th–17th-century Europe).
