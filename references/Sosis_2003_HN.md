# Sosis (2003) Why Aren't We All Hutterites? Costly Signaling Theory and Religious Behavior

#type/paper #theme/costly_signaling #theme/ritual #theme/cooperation #theme/hbe #theme/evolution_of_religion #journal/HN #theory/costly_signaling #theory/signaling_theory_religion #theory/cognitive_dissonance #theory/self_perception_theory

**Bibliography**: Sosis, Richard. 2003. "Why Aren't We All Hutterites? Costly Signaling Theory and Religious Behavior." *Human Nature* 14(2):91–127.

## Topic

This paper proposes a proximate psychological explanation for how individuals can pay the short-term costs of ritual performance in order to achieve the long-term fitness benefits offered by religious groups. Building on Irons's (1996a, 1996b, 1996c, 2001) verbal argument that religious rituals serve as costly signals of commitment to a group, the author develops a graphical model that addresses three unresolved ambiguities in that theory: why skeptics do not join high-benefit religious groups, how costly a ritual must be to prevent free-riders, and why religious groups mandate private ritual performance that cannot be enforced.

## Key Theory / Framework

The paper applies the **costly signaling theory of ritual**, which holds that religious behaviors are costly-to-fake signals of an individual's commitment to a religious group. Honest signaling is enabled because the costs of ritual performance are perceived differently by believers and skeptics. The author draws on two bodies of social-psychological research — **self-perception theory** (Bem, 1965, 1972) and **cognitive dissonance theory** (Festinger, 1957, 1964) — to argue that ritual performance can induce genuine belief. When individuals perform ritual actions associated with overt group values, either self-perception or cognitive dissonance processes cause nonbelievers to either modify their beliefs or discontinue the actions.

The key theoretical move is to decompose signal costs into *resource costs* (time, energy, material — equal for believers and skeptics performing the same actions) and *perceived opportunity costs* (the subjective valuation of alternatives forgone — higher for skeptics because their feasible alternative set is larger). A true believer who genuinely fears eternal damnation for breaking a fast perceives a very small alternative set; a skeptic's alternative set is unrestricted, so each additional hour of the fast becomes increasingly costly. This differential in perceived opportunity costs is what maintains the honest signaling equilibrium, not the absolute resource costs of ritual.

The model also addresses commitment problems from behavioral ecology (Frank, 1988; Schelling, 1960) and draws on Iannaccone's (1992, 1994) economic analyses of religious groups to frame the free-rider problem that rituals must solve.

## Data & Methods

This is a theoretical paper presenting a **graphical model**. There is no empirical data collection. The model is constructed across four figures:

- **Figure 1**: Net benefits of group membership for believers vs. skeptics as a function of investment; believers attain higher net benefits because they pay lower monitoring and punishment costs.
- **Figure 2**: Perceived opportunity cost isoclines as a function of ritual intensity; isoclines are flat for believers and steep for skeptics, so the perceived optimal ritual intensity is higher for believers (R_b > R_s).
- **Figure 3**: Perceived costs and benefits of belonging to a religious group as a function of ritual intensity; the minimum ritual requirement R_min determines group membership; fundamentalism characterized as increasing R_min.
- **Figure 4**: Formal conditions for stable costly rituals. Rituals are stable when:

  > C_ru + C_rv < B_r(i) − B_s(i) < C_su

  where C_ru = cumulative perceived cost to believers of public rituals, C_rv = perceived cost to believers of private rituals, C_su = cumulative perceived cost to skeptics of public rituals, B_r(i) = benefit of belonging to believers as a function of investment i, B_s(i) = benefit of belonging to skeptics.

The minimum ritual requirement for group membership is determined not by the costs to committed members but by the perceived costs to potential free-riders. Ritual requirements are expected to escalate until it is no longer advantageous for a skeptic to join, but remain below the threshold at which believers perceive negative net gains.

## Key Findings

### Finding 1: Skeptics do not join high-benefit religious groups because ritual induces belief

The model explains the title puzzle — why outsiders do not simply perform Hutterite rituals to obtain their exceptional reproductive benefits — through the relationship between ritual and belief. Non-believers who perform costly rituals either become believers (via self-perception or cognitive dissonance) or perceive opportunity costs so high that they discontinue the rituals. Firm nonbelievers who persist in performing rituals without belief change will evaluate the opportunity costs of ritual participation as greater than the difference in benefits between joining believers and joining skeptics. Thus, the stable equilibrium is one in which skeptics do not attempt to join religious groups: "We are not Hutterites because we do not believe in the teachings of the Hutterites, and the only way to perceive the net in-group benefits of the Hutterites is to truly believe in their way of life" (p. 116).

### Finding 2: Stability conditions formalized for the first time

The graphical model provides the first formal statement of how costly a ritual must be to be stable. Two conditions must simultaneously hold. First, the perceived cost of ritual performance for a skeptic (C_su) must exceed the benefit advantage of belonging to the believers over the skeptics: C_su > B_r(i) − B_s(i). Second, the perceived cost to a believer (C_ru + C_rv) must be less than that same benefit advantage: C_ru + C_rv < B_r(i) − B_s(i). When both conditions are met, ritual requirements stabilize in a population.

### Finding 3: Private rituals increase the stability of the entire signaling system

Because skeptics do not perform private rituals (there is no enforcement mechanism, and the model assumes they will not), only believers bear the costs of private ritual. This cost asymmetry forces the perceived cost gap between believers and skeptics for public rituals to be larger than it would otherwise be. The existence of private rituals therefore increases the stability of the entire suite of ritual requirements. Private rituals also function as a self-commitment device: an individual who voluntarily engages in unobserved private rituals "cannot rationalize such actions as coercion by group members" (p. 110) and thereby signals to herself that she is genuinely committed.

### Finding 4: Ritual has four features that promote belief via psychological mechanisms

The author identifies four characteristics of ritual that enable it to promote cosmological belief: (1) ritual involves physical actions, (2) is generally social and publicly performed, (3) is formal, and (4) is often repetitive and cyclical. Each characteristic contributes to cognitive dissonance or self-perception processes for performers who do not already share the values encoded in the ritual. The multimodality of ritual requirements — engaging the full range of human sensory systems — prevents practitioners from partially reconciling behavior with belief and forces a more complete attitudinal change.

### Finding 5: Belief is a proximate mechanism enabling adaptive ritual behavior

The model's theoretical conclusion is that belief in religious doctrine is not merely a cultural epiphenomenon but a proximate mechanism that makes costly signaling equilibria possible: "Beliefs are a proximate mechanism that facilitates the production of adaptive ritual behaviors" (p. 117). The direction of causation is bidirectional: ritual fosters belief, and belief lowers the perceived cost of ritual, closing the loop. This deviates from standard behavioral ecological models that assume actors can accurately assess material costs and benefits.

### Finding 6: Five testable predictions derived

The model generates the following predictions: (1) *Risk of free-riders* — costly signals will be more frequent when the risk of free-riders is high (groups not isolated, morphological traits unrelated to group membership, high in-group benefits); (2) *Early indoctrination* — groups with costly signals will invest heavily in early indoctrination, which minimizes the opportunity costs perceived by members; (3) *Converts* — converts will be trusted less than in-born members in groups with high levels of costly signaling, because converts perceive higher opportunity costs; (4) *Apostasy* — costliness of ritual requirements should be positively correlated with apostasy rates among newcomers; (5) *Proselytization* — proselytization should be less frequent among groups offering greater in-group benefits, though the relationship is moderated by economies of scale in membership. Hutterite retention rates (~98% of in-born members; Peter, 1987) and data on Shaker apostasy (Bainbridge, 1984) are cited as supporting illustrations.
